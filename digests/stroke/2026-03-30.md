**Rehabilitation**

Home based, tailored intervention to reduce rate of falls after stroke (FAST): randomised trial. *BMJ*. [41876122](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41876122/)

**Research Question:** Does a multidisciplinary, home-based intervention combining functional exercise, home hazard reduction, and community mobility coaching reduce falls in community-dwelling stroke survivors?

A 6-month tailored physiotherapy and occupational therapy intervention reduced the fall rate by 33% over 12 months (IRR 0.67, 95% CI 0.48–0.94) in ambulatory stroke survivors, with clinically meaningful gains in walking speed, balance, self-efficacy, and community participation.

**Details:**
- Design: Multicentre RCT, N=370, community-dwelling adults >50 years, within 5 years of stroke, able to walk 10 m, across three Australian states
- Primary outcome: Rate of falls at 12 months — IRR 0.67 (95% CI 0.48–0.94; P=0.02), a 33% reduction; no significant difference in proportion of fallers (absolute risk reduction 0.03, 95% CI −0.07 to 0.13; P=0.52)
- Limitations: Reduction in fall rate without a significant reduction in the proportion of fallers raises questions about whether a subset of participants drove the effect; exclusion of those with moderate-to-severe receptive aphasia or fast walkers without prior falls limits generalisability to higher-functioning or more cognitively impaired survivors

**Rehabilitation**

Robot-Assisted Therapy for Upper Limb Rehabilitation After Stroke: Umbrella Review. *Journal of Medical Internet Research*. [41879816](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41879816/)

**Research Question:** Does robot-assisted therapy improve upper limb outcomes after stroke compared to conventional therapy, and do effects vary by stroke stage, impairment level, or robot type?

Robot-assisted therapy consistently improves upper limb motor function compared to conventional therapy, but this does not translate into significant gains in activities of daily living — meaning functional recovery at the body level is not yet reaching the participation level that matters most to patients.

**Details:**
- Design: Umbrella review of 21 meta-analyses (535 RCTs, 27,598 patients); acute, subacute, and chronic stroke populations; AMSTAR 2 quality assessment (17 high quality, 3 moderate, 1 critically low)
- Primary outcome: RAT superior for upper limb motor function; no statistically significant advantage over conventional therapy for activities of daily living
- Limitations: Significant outcome heterogeneity across included meta-analyses and methodological constraints inherent to umbrella reviews limit the strength of subgroup-level clinical guidance

**Rehabilitation**

Effect of noninvasive brain stimulation for unilateral spatial neglect after stroke: a systematic review and meta-analysis. *Journal of Neurology*. [41880010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41880010/)

**Research Question:** Does noninvasive brain stimulation (NIBS) improve unilateral spatial neglect after stroke, and which modality and protocol optimise outcomes?

rTMS and TBS both produced large, significant reductions in neglect severity across multiple standardised tests, while tDCS showed smaller but significant effects and tACS showed no benefit — with earlier intervention and excitatory stimulation of the ipsilesional posterior parietal cortex yielding the largest gains.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 17 RCTs/crossover RCTs, N = 425 patients with post-stroke unilateral spatial neglect
- Primary outcome: rTMS reduced line bisection test (SMD −1.82), star cancellation test (SMD −1.59), and Catherine Bergego Scale (MD −2.76); TBS reduced SCT (SMD −2.59) and CBS (MD −0.93); tDCS reduced LBT (SMD −0.60) and SCT (SMD −0.60); tACS showed no significant effect on either measure
- Limitations: Small total sample (425 patients across 17 studies) with considerable heterogeneity in stroke phase, stimulation parameters, and outcome timing, limiting confidence in pooled effect sizes and direct head-to-head comparisons between modalities

**Acute Treatment**

Effectiveness and safety of endovascular therapy compared to intravenous thrombolysis in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusions: a real-world multicentre study from the Italian SITS registry. *European Stroke Journal*. [41904677](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41904677/)

**Research Question:** Does endovascular therapy (with or without IVT) improve 90-day functional outcomes compared to IVT alone in patients with medium-vessel occlusion stroke in routine clinical practice?

EVT offered no functional benefit over IVT alone in MeVO stroke (60.6% vs 60.9% independent at 90 days) but was associated with significantly higher rates of intracranial haemorrhage (18.4% vs 11.2%), reinforcing a cautious approach to EVT in this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective multicentre registry study (SITS registry, 82 Italian centres, 2020–2023), N=780 after 1:1 propensity score matching (390 per group)
- Primary outcome: Functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days — 60.6% EVT±IVT vs 60.9% IVT alone (OR 0.99; 95% CI 0.73–1.34; P=.939)
- Limitations: Retrospective registry design with incomplete procedural data and anatomical heterogeneity in MeVO classification limits causal inference and generalisability

**Prevention**

Discrepant Outcomes of Direct Oral Anticoagulant Trials in Device-Detected Atrial Fibrillation after Stroke: Insights from NOAH-AFNET 6 and ARTESIA. *Clinical and Applied Thrombosis/Hemostasis*. [41877615](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41877615/)

**Research Question:** Should patients with device-detected atrial fibrillation and a prior stroke or TIA receive direct oral anticoagulants?

In the highest-risk subgroup (prior stroke/TIA), apixaban reduced ischemic stroke in ARTESIA but NOAH-AFNET 6 showed only a non-significant stroke reduction alongside substantial bleeding increase — leaving clinicians without a clear net-benefit signal to guide anticoagulation decisions.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review synthesizing two RCTs (NOAH-AFNET 6 and ARTESIA); prior stroke/TIA subgroups analyzed
- Primary outcome: ARTESIA — apixaban reduced ischemic stroke with increased major bleeding (net signal favourable in prior stroke/TIA subgroup); NOAH-AFNET 6 — modest, non-significant ischemic stroke reduction with substantial major bleeding increase
- Limitations: Subgroup analyses from two discordant trials with differing DOAC agents, AF burden thresholds, and background aspirin use limit direct comparison and generalisability

**Prevention**

CREST-2: Early reflections on the primary results. *Seminars in Vascular Surgery*. [41866678](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41866678/)

**Research Question:** In patients with high-grade asymptomatic carotid stenosis receiving intensive modern medical therapy, does carotid revascularization provide additional stroke-prevention benefit?

Carotid endarterectomy showed numerically better stroke prevention than intensive medical management alone at 4 years but did not reach statistical significance, while carotid stenting demonstrated improved periprocedural safety under strict credentialing — reinforcing intensive medical management as the foundation but keeping revascularization relevant in selected patients with experienced operators.

**Details:**
- Design: Randomized controlled trial (review/early reflections on primary results); population restricted to patients with favorable vascular anatomy and high-grade asymptomatic carotid stenosis receiving protocol-driven intensive medical management
- Primary outcome: Carotid endarterectomy showed numerically favorable stroke reduction vs. intensive medical management at 4 years, but the difference did not reach statistical significance; carotid stenting met improved periprocedural safety benchmarks compared with earlier multicenter experiences
- Limitations: Trial execution spanned a period of rapid evolution in risk-factor management and device technology, making it difficult to isolate the effect of revascularization from contemporaneous improvements in medical care; enrollment was restricted to favorable anatomy, limiting generalizability

**Prevention**

Nonoperative management of patients with symptomatic carotid stenosis. *Seminars in Vascular Surgery*. [41866676](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41866676/)

**Research Question:** In patients with symptomatic carotid stenosis, can intensive medical therapy alone achieve outcomes comparable to revascularization in appropriately selected low-risk patients?

Intensive medical therapy — combining antiplatelets, aggressive lipid lowering, blood pressure control, and lifestyle modification — appears to be a safe and effective alternative to revascularization in symptomatic carotid stenosis patients who lack high-risk plaque features, potentially reshaping how treatment decisions are made.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review; no primary patient cohort
- Primary outcome: Qualitative synthesis showing that absence of high-risk plaque features (intraplaque hemorrhage, large lipid-rich necrotic core, thin fibrous cap, plaque ulceration, vessel wall enhancement, microembolic signals on TCD) supports medical management; interim data from ECST-2 and CASCOM suggest comparable recurrent stroke outcomes with medical therapy in selected patients
- Limitations: As a review without pooled meta-analytic data, conclusions rest on heterogeneous trial populations and interim results; definitive guidance awaits final outcomes from ongoing RCTs

**Rehabilitation**

Effects of Neuromuscular Electrical Stimulation on Swallowing Function for Post-Stroke Dysphagia: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis. *International Journal of Language & Communication Disorders*. [41888972](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41888972/)

**Research Question:** Does adding neuromuscular electrical stimulation (NMES) to conventional swallowing therapy improve dysphagia outcomes after stroke compared to therapy without NMES?

NMES combined with swallowing therapy significantly reduced aspiration risk (Penetration Aspiration Scale MD −1.05, 95% CI −1.50 to −0.60) and improved functional swallowing scores (FDS MD −7.23, 95% CI −13.62 to −0.84), but showed no significant benefit on functional oral intake — supporting NMES as a useful adjunct rather than a standalone treatment.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 16 RCTs, post-stroke dysphagia populations
- Primary outcome: Penetration Aspiration Scale MD −1.05 (95% CI −1.50 to −0.60, P < 0.00001); Functional Dysphagia Scale MD −7.23 (95% CI −13.62 to −0.84, P = 0.03); Functional Oral Intake Scale MD 0.66 (95% CI −0.31 to 1.62, P = 0.18, non-significant)
- Limitations: Only 16 studies included, with acknowledged methodological limitations and heterogeneity across trials; stimulation parameters varied considerably, limiting guidance on optimal dosing

**Hospital Care**

Functional Outcome Following Blood Pressure Drops Within 24 Hours After Successful Mechanical Thrombectomy. *Neurology*. [41886712](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41886712/)

**Research Question:** Do systolic blood pressure drops of ≥40 mmHg within 24 hours after successful mechanical thrombectomy worsen functional outcomes at 3 months?

Postprocedural BP drops occurred in 41% of patients after successful thrombectomy and were independently associated with worse 3-month disability (adjusted OR 1.66) and more than double the rate of early neurologic deterioration — suggesting post-MT hemodynamic stability deserves the same attention as intra-procedural BP management.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective observational multicenter study, N=300, acute ischemic stroke patients with successful recanalization (mTICI ≥2b) from 2 German tertiary stroke centers, 2020–2022
- Primary outcome: mRS shift analysis at 3 months — BP drops (any) associated with worse functional outcome (adjusted common OR 1.66, 95% CI 1.02–2.70); each additional drop carried adjusted common OR 1.33 (95% CI 1.05–1.69); early neurologic deterioration 22.2% vs 13.4% (adjusted OR 2.22, 95% CI 1.03–4.78)
- Limitations: Retrospective design with only 2 centers limits generalizability, and the observational nature cannot exclude residual confounding — BP drops may be a marker of underlying instability rather than an independent causal mechanism
