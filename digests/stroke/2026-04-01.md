**Rehabilitation**

Home based, tailored intervention to reduce rate of falls after stroke (FAST): randomised trial. *BMJ*. [41876122](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41876122/)

**Research Question:** Does a multidisciplinary, home-based intervention combining functional exercise, fall hazard reduction, and community mobility coaching reduce falls in community-dwelling stroke survivors?

A 6-month tailored physiotherapy and occupational therapy intervention reduced the rate of falls by 33% over 12 months in ambulatory stroke survivors, with accompanying improvements in walking speed, balance, self-efficacy, and community participation.

**Details:**
- Design: Multicentre RCT, N=370, community-dwelling adults >50 years within 5 years of stroke, able to walk 10 m, Australia
- Primary outcome: Rate of falls at 12 months — IRR 0.67 (95% CI 0.48–0.94; P=0.02), a 33% reduction; no significant difference in proportion of fallers (ARR 0.03, 95% CI −0.07 to 0.13; P=0.52)
- Limitations: Reduction in fall rate without a corresponding reduction in the proportion of fallers suggests the benefit may reflect fewer repeat falls in a subgroup rather than broad fall prevention; exclusion of those with moderate-to-severe receptive aphasia or fast walkers without recent falls limits generalisability to higher-risk or more severely impaired patients

**Rehabilitation**

Robot-Assisted Therapy for Upper Limb Rehabilitation After Stroke: Umbrella Review. *Journal of Medical Internet Research*. [41879816](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41879816/)

**Research Question:** Does robot-assisted therapy improve upper limb function and activities of daily living after stroke compared with conventional therapy?

Robot-assisted therapy improves upper limb motor function across all stroke stages, but does not translate into statistically significant gains in activities of daily living — meaning functional recovery at the body level is not yet reaching meaningful independence in daily life.

**Details:**
- Design: Umbrella review of 21 meta-analyses, 535 RCTs, N = 27,598 patients across acute, subacute, and chronic stroke stages
- Primary outcome: RAT superior to conventional therapy for upper limb motor function; no statistically significant advantage in ADLs; treatment effects moderated by stroke stage, impairment severity, and robot type
- Limitations: Significant outcome heterogeneity across included meta-analyses and methodological limitations inherent to umbrella review design limit the strength of subgroup conclusions

**Acute Treatment**

Effectiveness and safety of endovascular therapy compared to intravenous thrombolysis in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusions: a real-world multicentre study from the Italian SITS registry. *European Stroke Journal*. [41904677](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41904677/)

**Research Question:** Does endovascular therapy (with or without IVT) improve functional outcomes compared to IVT alone in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusion (MeVO) in real-world practice?

EVT offered no functional benefit over IVT alone in MeVO stroke (mRS 0–2 at 90 days: 60.6% vs 60.9%, OR 0.99) but increased overall haemorrhagic complications (18.4% vs 11.2%), supporting a cautious approach to routine EVT in this setting.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective multicentre registry study (SITS registry, 82 Italian centres, 2020–2023), propensity score–matched cohort, N = 780 (390 per group)
- Primary outcome: Functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days — 60.6% EVT ± IVT vs 60.9% IVT alone (OR 0.99; 95% CI 0.73–1.34; P = .939)
- Limitations: Retrospective registry design with incomplete procedural data and anatomical heterogeneity in MeVO classification limits causal inference and generalisability

**Acute Treatment**

Functional Outcome Following Blood Pressure Drops Within 24 Hours After Successful Mechanical Thrombectomy. *Neurology*. [41886712](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41886712/)

**Research Question:** Do systolic blood pressure drops of ≥40 mmHg within 24 hours after successful mechanical thrombectomy independently worsen functional outcome at 3 months?

Post-thrombectomy BP drops occurred in 41% of patients and were independently associated with worse 3-month disability (adjusted OR 1.66) and more than double the rate of early neurologic deterioration, suggesting active BP stabilization after recanalization warrants formal investigation.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective multicenter observational study, N=300, acute ischemic stroke patients with successful recanalization (mTICI ≥2b) from 2 tertiary centers in the German Stroke Registry
- Primary outcome: mRS shift analysis at 3 months — BP drops associated with worse functional outcome (any drop vs. none: adjusted common OR 1.66, 95% CI 1.02–2.70; per additional drop: adjusted common OR 1.33, 95% CI 1.05–1.69); early neurologic deterioration 22.2% vs. 13.4% (adjusted OR 2.22, 95% CI 1.03–4.78)
- Limitations: Retrospective design with unmeasured confounding; BP drop definition (≥40 mmHg within 1 hour) is non-standard and etiology of drops (e.g., sedation, medication, cardiac) was not systematically adjudicated, limiting causal inference

**Rehabilitation**

Effect of noninvasive brain stimulation for unilateral spatial neglect after stroke: a systematic review and meta-analysis. *Journal of Neurology*. [41880010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41880010/)

**Research Question:** Does noninvasive brain stimulation (NIBS) improve unilateral spatial neglect after stroke, and which modality and protocol yield the greatest benefit?

rTMS and TBS produced large reductions in neglect severity across multiple validated measures, outperforming tDCS and tACS, with early intervention and excitatory stimulation of the ipsilesional posterior parietal cortex associated with the greatest effect — offering actionable guidance for clinical NIBS protocols.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 17 RCTs/crossover RCTs, N = 425 patients
- Primary outcome: rTMS reduced line bisection error (SMD = −1.82), star cancellation (SMD = −1.59), and Catherine Bergego Scale (MD = −2.76); TBS reduced star cancellation (SMD = −2.59) and CBS (MD = −0.93); tDCS showed modest effects (SMD ≈ −0.60 for both tests); tACS showed no significant benefit
- Limitations: Small total sample (425 patients across 17 studies) limits precision of subgroup estimates, and heterogeneity in stimulation parameters, stroke etiology, and neglect severity across trials reduces confidence in the moderator analyses

**Prevention**

Discrepant Outcomes of Direct Oral Anticoagulant Trials in Device-Detected Atrial Fibrillation after Stroke: Insights from NOAH-AFNET 6 and ARTESIA. *Clinical and Applied Thrombosis/Hemostasis*. [41877615](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41877615/)

**Research Question:** Should patients with device-detected atrial fibrillation and prior stroke or TIA receive direct oral anticoagulants for secondary stroke prevention?

In the highest-risk subgroup (prior stroke/TIA), apixaban reduced ischemic stroke in ARTESIA while edoxaban showed only a non-significant reduction in NOAH-AFNET 6, but both trials demonstrated increased major bleeding — leaving the benefit-risk balance unresolved and patient selection critical.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review synthesizing two RCTs — ARTESIA (apixaban vs. aspirin) and NOAH-AFNET 6 (edoxaban vs. placebo/aspirin) in patients with subclinical/device-detected AF
- Primary outcome: ARTESIA showed ischemic stroke reduction with apixaban alongside increased major bleeding; NOAH-AFNET 6 showed a modest, non-significant ischemic stroke reduction with a substantial increase in major bleeding in the prior stroke/TIA subgroup
- Limitations: Subgroup analyses in both trials were not powered for definitive conclusions; trial designs, comparators, and AF burden thresholds differed, limiting direct comparison

No-reflow phenomenon after vessel recanalization in acute ischemic stroke: mechanisms, diagnosis, and treatment directions. *Journal of Neurology*. [41915244](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41915244/)

Despite successful large-vessel recanalization, persistent microvascular failure (no-reflow) affects 3.3–63% of reperfused stroke patients and substantially worsens outcomes, yet no standardized treatment exists.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review; N/A; acute ischemic stroke patients undergoing reperfusion therapy
- Primary outcome: No-reflow prevalence ranged from 3.3% to 63% post-reperfusion; driven by distal embolization, microthrombus formation, inflammation, cerebral edema, and neurovascular unit dysfunction
- Limitations: Narrative review without systematic methodology or meta-analysis; wide prevalence range reflects heterogeneous diagnostic criteria across studies, limiting definitive conclusions about incidence and treatment efficacy

**Imaging**

Comparative Accuracy of TCD, TTE, TEE, and Cardiac CT in Detecting Right-to-Left Shunt in Embolic Stroke of Undetermined Source. *Neurology: Clinical Practice*. [41908736](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41908736/)

**Research Question:** Which imaging modality — TCD, TTE, TEE, or cardiac CT — performs best for detecting right-to-left shunt in patients with embolic stroke of undetermined source?

TCD offers 100% sensitivity and NPV for RLS detection, making it the optimal noninvasive first-line screen in ESUS, while TEE provides the highest overall accuracy (91.3%) for confirmation; cardiac CT, despite perfect specificity, misses over half of cases and should not be used alone.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective comparative study, N=130 (from 469 screened), ESUS patients undergoing ≥3 of 4 modalities at a single neurosonology center
- Primary outcome: TCD sensitivity 100%, specificity 75.0%, accuracy 87.6%; TEE sensitivity 95.1%, specificity 87.5%, accuracy 91.3%; TTE sensitivity 83.3%, specificity 93.3%, accuracy 88.1%; cardiac CT sensitivity 45.8%, specificity 100%; no significant AUC difference among TCD, TTE, and TEE, but all three outperformed cardiac CT (p<0.05)
- Limitations: Class IV evidence with significant referral and selection bias in a single-center retrospective cohort; PFO confirmation was defined by concordance of ≥2 tests rather than an independent gold standard, which may artificially inflate TCD's apparent sensitivity

**Imaging**

Automated Intracranial Hemorrhage Detection: Real-World Experience in a Large Comprehensive Stroke Center. *Neurology: Clinical Practice*. [41908735](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41908735/)

**Research Question:** How does automated ICH detection software perform against radiologist interpretation in real-world code stroke patients?

Automated ICH software achieved 81% sensitivity and 99% specificity overall, but sensitivity for isolated extra-axial hemorrhage was only 43% — a clinically important blind spot that warrants caution when subdural or epidural hematoma is on the differential.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective cohort, N=1,402 code stroke noncontrast CT scans, single comprehensive stroke center, January 2022–February 2023
- Primary outcome: Sensitivity 81% (95% CI 74%–88%), specificity 99% (98%–99%), PPV 85%, NPV 98% for ICH detection; sensitivity 94% for intra-axial hemorrhage vs. 43% for isolated extra-axial hemorrhage
- Limitations: Single-center retrospective design limits generalizability; radiologist reports used as gold standard without independent blinded re-read; 32 scans (2%) excluded due to motion artifact

**Rehabilitation**

Effects of Neuromuscular Electrical Stimulation on Swallowing Function for Post-Stroke Dysphagia: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis. *International Journal of Language & Communication Disorders*. [41888972](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41888972/)

**Research Question:** Does adding neuromuscular electrical stimulation (NMES) to conventional swallowing therapy improve dysphagia outcomes in stroke survivors compared to conventional therapy alone?

NMES combined with conventional swallowing therapy significantly reduced aspiration risk (Penetration Aspiration Scale MD −1.05, 95% CI −1.50 to −0.60) and improved functional swallowing scores (FDS MD −7.23), but did not significantly improve oral intake scale scores, and NMES alone has not been shown to outperform conventional therapy.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 16 RCTs, post-stroke dysphagia populations
- Primary outcome: Penetration Aspiration Scale MD −1.05 (95% CI −1.50 to −0.60, P < 0.00001); Functional Dysphagia Scale MD −7.23 (95% CI −13.62 to −0.84, P = 0.03); Functional Oral Intake Scale MD 0.66 (95% CI −0.31 to 1.62, P = 0.18, non-significant)
- Limitations: Only 16 studies included with acknowledged methodological heterogeneity and limited evidence base; stimulation parameters varied across trials, limiting generalisability of optimal dosing recommendations
