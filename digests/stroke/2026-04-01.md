**Rehabilitation**

Home based, tailored intervention to reduce rate of falls after stroke (FAST): randomised trial. *BMJ*. [41876122](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41876122/)

**Research Question:** Does a multidisciplinary, home-based intervention combining functional exercise, fall hazard reduction, and community mobility coaching reduce falls in community-dwelling stroke survivors?

A 6-month tailored physiotherapy and occupational therapy intervention reduced the rate of falls by 33% over 12 months in ambulatory stroke survivors, with accompanying improvements in walking speed, balance, self-efficacy, and community participation.

**Details:**
- Design: Multicentre RCT, N=370, community-dwelling adults >50 years within 5 years of stroke, able to walk 10 m, across 3 Australian states
- Primary outcome: Fall rate at 12 months — IRR 0.67 (95% CI 0.48–0.94; P=0.02), representing a 33% reduction; no significant difference in proportion of fallers (ARR 0.03, 95% CI −0.07 to 0.13; P=0.52)
- Limitations: Reduction in fall rate without a significant reduction in the proportion of fallers suggests the benefit may be concentrated in repeat fallers; exclusion of those with moderate-to-severe receptive aphasia and fast walkers without recent falls limits generalisability to higher-functioning survivors

**Rehabilitation**

Robot-Assisted Therapy for Upper Limb Rehabilitation After Stroke: Umbrella Review. *Journal of Medical Internet Research*. [41879816](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41879816/)

**Research Question:** Does robot-assisted therapy improve upper limb function and daily activities after stroke compared with conventional therapy?

Robot-assisted therapy improves upper limb motor function across stroke stages, but this does not reliably translate into gains in activities of daily living — meaning functional benefits remain largely at the impairment level.

**Details:**
- Design: Umbrella review of 21 meta-analyses, 535 RCTs, N = 27,598 patients across acute, subacute, and chronic stroke stages
- Primary outcome: RAT superior for upper limb motor function; no statistically significant advantage over conventional therapy for ADLs
- Limitations: Significant outcome heterogeneity across included meta-analyses and methodological constraints inherent to umbrella reviews limit the strength of subgroup-level conclusions

**Acute Treatment**

Effectiveness and safety of endovascular therapy compared to intravenous thrombolysis in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusions: a real-world multicentre study from the Italian SITS registry. *European Stroke Journal*. [41904677](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41904677/)

**Research Question:** Does endovascular therapy (with or without IVT) improve functional outcomes compared to IVT alone in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusion in routine clinical practice?

EVT offered no functional benefit over IVT alone in MeVO stroke (mRS 0–2 at 90 days: 60.6% vs 60.9%) but increased overall haemorrhagic complications nearly twofold, reinforcing a cautious approach to routine EVT in this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective multicentre registry study (82 Italian SITS centres, 2020–2023), propensity score–matched cohort, N = 780 (390 per group)
- Primary outcome: Functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days — 60.6% EVT ± IVT vs 60.9% IVT only (OR 0.99; 95% CI 0.73–1.34; P = .939)
- Limitations: Retrospective registry design with incomplete procedural data, anatomical heterogeneity in MeVO classification, and inability to fully account for unmeasured confounders

**Acute Treatment**

Functional Outcome Following Blood Pressure Drops Within 24 Hours After Successful Mechanical Thrombectomy. *Neurology*. [41886712](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41886712/)

**Research Question:** Do systolic blood pressure drops of ≥40 mmHg within the first 24 hours after successful mechanical thrombectomy independently worsen functional outcome at 3 months?

Post-thrombectomy BP instability is actionable: each additional systolic drop of ≥40 mmHg within 24 hours after successful recanalization independently worsened 3-month disability (adjusted OR 1.33 per drop) and more than doubled the odds of early neurologic deterioration, making BP monitoring and stabilization an immediate priority in stroke unit care.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective observational multicenter study, N=300, acute ischemic stroke patients with successful recanalization (mTICI ≥2b) at 2 German tertiary stroke centers, October 2020–September 2022
- Primary outcome: mRS shift at 3 months — BP drops associated with worse functional outcome (any drop vs. none: adjusted common OR 1.66, 95% CI 1.02–2.70; per additional drop: adjusted common OR 1.33, 95% CI 1.05–1.69); early neurologic deterioration 22.2% vs. 13.4% (adjusted OR 2.22, 95% CI 1.03–4.78)
- Limitations: Retrospective design with inherent confounding; BP drops may be a marker of underlying physiological instability (e.g., cardiac events, sepsis) rather than an independent cause of harm, limiting causal inference

**Rehabilitation**

Effect of noninvasive brain stimulation for unilateral spatial neglect after stroke: a systematic review and meta-analysis. *Journal of Neurology*. [41880010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41880010/)

**Research Question:** Does noninvasive brain stimulation (NIBS) improve unilateral spatial neglect after stroke, and which modality and protocol are most effective?

rTMS and TBS produced the largest reductions in neglect severity across standardized assessments, with early intervention and excitatory stimulation of the ipsilesional posterior parietal cortex outperforming other approaches — suggesting clinicians should consider both timing and target when incorporating NIBS into neglect rehabilitation.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 17 RCTs or crossover RCTs, N = 425 patients with post-stroke unilateral spatial neglect
- Primary outcome: rTMS reduced line bisection test (SMD = −1.82), star cancellation test (SMD = −1.59), and Catherine Bergego Scale (MD = −2.76); TBS reduced star cancellation test (SMD = −2.59) and CBS (MD = −0.93); tDCS showed smaller but significant effects (SMD ≈ −0.60 on both LBT and SCT); tACS showed no significant benefit
- Limitations: Only 17 small trials (total N = 425) with likely heterogeneity in stimulation parameters, stroke etiology, and neglect severity, limiting confidence in precise effect size estimates and generalizability

**Prevention**

Discrepant Outcomes of Direct Oral Anticoagulant Trials in Device-Detected Atrial Fibrillation after Stroke: Insights from NOAH-AFNET 6 and ARTESIA. *Clinical and Applied Thrombosis/Hemostasis*. [41877615](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41877615/)

**Research Question:** Should patients with device-detected atrial fibrillation and a prior stroke or TIA receive direct oral anticoagulants for secondary stroke prevention?

In the highest-risk subgroup, apixaban (ARTESIA) reduced ischemic stroke but increased major bleeding, while edoxaban (NOAH-AFNET 6) showed only a non-significant stroke reduction with a substantial bleeding increase — leaving clinicians without a clear consensus signal to guide anticoagulation decisions in this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review synthesizing two RCTs — NOAH-AFNET 6 and ARTESIA — in patients with subclinical/device-detected AF, including subgroups with prior stroke or TIA
- Primary outcome: ARTESIA — apixaban reduced ischemic stroke with increased major bleeding in the prior stroke/TIA subgroup; NOAH-AFNET 6 — edoxaban showed modest, non-significant ischemic stroke reduction with substantial major bleeding increase
- Limitations: Subgroup analyses from two trials with discordant results and differing designs; evidence base is limited and internally inconsistent, precluding firm treatment recommendations

No-reflow phenomenon after vessel recanalization in acute ischemic stroke: mechanisms, diagnosis, and treatment directions. *Journal of Neurology*. [41915244](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41915244/)

**Research Question:** What are the mechanisms, diagnostic approaches, and treatment strategies for the no-reflow phenomenon in acute ischemic stroke patients who achieve successful large-vessel recanalization?

No-reflow phenomenon affects 3.3–63% of patients after successful recanalization and worsens clinical outcomes, yet targeted therapies addressing its underlying microvascular mechanisms — including distal embolization, microthrombus formation, pericyte dysfunction, and neurovascular unit disruption — remain incompletely defined and largely investigational.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review; N/A; AIS patients undergoing reperfusion therapy
- Primary outcome: Prevalence of NRP ranges from 3.3% to 63% after reperfusion therapy; associated with significantly increased risk of poor clinical outcomes
- Limitations: As a narrative review, it does not systematically pool data or assess evidence quality, limiting the strength of therapeutic recommendations drawn from heterogeneous studies

**Imaging**

Automated Intracranial Hemorrhage Detection: Real-World Experience in a Large Comprehensive Stroke Center. *Neurology: Clinical Practice*. [41908735](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41908735/)

**Research Question:** How does automated ICH detection software perform compared with board-certified radiologists on real-world noncontrast CT scans in a comprehensive stroke center?

Automated ICH detection achieved 81% overall sensitivity and 99% specificity, but sensitivity for isolated extra-axial hemorrhage was only 43% — a critical blind spot when the software is used as a screening safety net.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective cohort, N=1,402 code stroke CT scans, single comprehensive stroke center, January 2022–February 2023
- Primary outcome: Sensitivity 81% (95% CI 74%–88%), specificity 99% (98%–99%), PPV 85%, NPV 98%; sensitivity was 94% for intra-axial hemorrhage but only 43% for isolated extra-axial hemorrhage
- Limitations: Single-center retrospective design limits generalizability; radiology report used as gold standard rather than independent blinded re-read; 32 scans (2%) excluded for motion artifact, potentially biasing results

**Rehabilitation**

Effects of Neuromuscular Electrical Stimulation on Swallowing Function for Post-Stroke Dysphagia: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis. *International Journal of Language & Communication Disorders*. [41888972](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41888972/)

**Research Question:** Does adding neuromuscular electrical stimulation (NMES) to conventional swallowing therapy improve dysphagia outcomes more than conventional therapy alone in post-stroke patients?

NMES combined with conventional swallowing therapy significantly reduced Penetration Aspiration Scale scores (MD −1.05, 95% CI −1.50 to −0.60) and improved Functional Dysphagia Scale scores (MD −7.23, 95% CI −13.62 to −0.84), supporting its integration into post-stroke dysphagia rehabilitation — though NMES alone does not appear superior to standard swallowing therapy.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 16 RCTs, post-stroke dysphagia populations
- Primary outcome: Penetration Aspiration Scale MD −1.05 (95% CI −1.50 to −0.60, P < 0.00001); Functional Dysphagia Scale MD −7.23 (95% CI −13.62 to −0.84, P = 0.03); Functional Oral Intake Scale non-significant (MD 0.66, P = 0.18)
- Limitations: Only 16 studies included with acknowledged methodological limitations across trials; heterogeneity in NMES parameters and comparator interventions limits generalisability of effect sizes

**Imaging**

Comparative Accuracy of TCD, TTE, TEE, and Cardiac CT in Detecting Right-to-Left Shunt in Embolic Stroke of Undetermined Source. *Neurology: Clinical Practice*. [41908736](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41908736/)

**Research Question:** Among patients with ESUS, which imaging modality — TCD, TTE, TEE, or cardiac CT — best detects right-to-left shunt due to PFO?

TCD offers 100% sensitivity and NPV for RLS detection, making it the ideal noninvasive first-line screen, while cardiac CT's perfect specificity but 45.8% sensitivity limits its standalone use.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective comparative study, N=130 ESUS patients who underwent ≥3 of 4 modalities at a single neurosonology centre
- Primary outcome: TCD sensitivity 100%, specificity 75.0%, accuracy 87.6%; TEE sensitivity 95.1%, specificity 87.5%, accuracy 91.3% (highest overall); TTE sensitivity 83.3%, specificity 93.3%, accuracy 88.1%; cardiac CT sensitivity 45.8%, specificity 100%; TCD, TTE, and TEE each outperformed cardiac CT by AUC (p<0.05), with no significant differences among the three
- Limitations: Class IV evidence from a single-centre retrospective design with significant referral and selection bias (130/469 screened patients included), limiting generalisability
