**Rehabilitation**

Home based, tailored intervention to reduce rate of falls after stroke (FAST): randomised trial. *BMJ*. [41876122](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41876122/)

**Research Question:** Does a 6-month multidisciplinary, home-based intervention combining functional exercise, fall hazard reduction, and community mobility coaching reduce falls in community-dwelling stroke survivors?

A tailored physiotherapist–occupational therapist intervention reduced fall rates by 33% over 12 months in ambulatory stroke survivors, with accompanying improvements in walking speed, balance, self-efficacy, and community participation.

**Details:**
- Design: Multicentre RCT, N=370, community-dwelling adults >50 years within 5 years of stroke, able to walk 10 m
- Primary outcome: Fall rate at 12 months — IRR 0.67 (95% CI 0.48–0.94; P=0.02), representing a 33% reduction; no significant difference in proportion of fallers (ARR 0.03, P=0.52)
- Limitations: Reduction in fall rate without a significant reduction in the proportion of fallers suggests the benefit may be driven by repeat fallers; exclusion of those with moderate-to-severe receptive aphasia and faster walkers limits generalisability to higher-functioning survivors

**Rehabilitation**

Robot-Assisted Therapy for Upper Limb Rehabilitation After Stroke: Umbrella Review. *Journal of Medical Internet Research*. [41879816](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41879816/)

**Research Question:** Does robot-assisted therapy improve upper limb motor function and activities of daily living after stroke compared with conventional therapy?

Robot-assisted therapy significantly improves upper limb motor function after stroke, but this does not reliably translate into gains in activities of daily living — clinicians should set realistic expectations about functional carryover.

**Details:**
- Design: Umbrella review of 21 meta-analyses, 535 RCTs, N = 27,598 patients across acute, subacute, and chronic stroke stages
- Primary outcome: RAT superior for upper limb motor function; no statistically significant advantage over conventional therapy for activities of daily living
- Limitations: Significant outcome heterogeneity across included reviews and methodological limitations inherent to umbrella review design limit precision of effect estimates and generalisability

**Rehabilitation**

Effect of noninvasive brain stimulation for unilateral spatial neglect after stroke: a systematic review and meta-analysis. *Journal of Neurology*. [41880010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41880010/)

**Research Question:** Does noninvasive brain stimulation (NIBS) improve unilateral spatial neglect after stroke, and which modality and protocol produce the largest effect?

rTMS and TBS reduced neglect severity on validated assessments with large effect sizes, outperforming tDCS and tACS, while early intervention and excitatory stimulation of the ipsilesional posterior parietal cortex amplified benefit — offering actionable guidance for clinical protocol selection.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 17 RCTs or crossover RCTs, N = 425 patients with post-stroke unilateral spatial neglect
- Primary outcome: rTMS reduced line bisection test (SMD = −1.82, P < 0.00001), star cancellation test (SMD = −1.59, P < 0.00001), and Catherine Bergego Scale (MD = −2.76, P = 0.002); TBS reduced star cancellation test (SMD = −2.59, P = 0.03) and CBS (MD = −0.93, P < 0.0001); tDCS showed smaller but significant effects on both paper-based tests; tACS showed no significant benefit
- Limitations: Small total sample (425 patients across 17 studies) with likely heterogeneity in lesion location, neglect severity, and stimulation parameters limits confidence in effect size estimates and generalizability

**Acute Treatment**

Effectiveness and safety of endovascular therapy compared to intravenous thrombolysis in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusions: a real-world multicentre study from the Italian SITS registry. *European Stroke Journal*. [41904677](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41904677/)

**Research Question:** Does endovascular therapy (with or without IVT) improve functional outcomes compared to IVT alone in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusion in real-world practice?

EVT offered no functional advantage over IVT alone in MeVO stroke (mRS 0–2 at 90 days: 60.6% vs 60.9%; OR 0.99) but carried a significantly higher rate of intracranial haemorrhage (18.4% vs 11.2%), reinforcing a cautious approach to routine EVT in this setting.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective multicentre registry study (SITS registry, 82 Italian centres, 2020–2023); propensity score–matched cohort, N = 780 (390 per group)
- Primary outcome: Functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days — 60.6% (EVT ± IVT) vs 60.9% (IVT alone); OR 0.99 (95% CI 0.73–1.34; P = .939)
- Limitations: Retrospective registry design with incomplete procedural data, anatomical heterogeneity in MeVO classification, and potential residual confounding despite propensity matching

**Hospital Care**

Functional Outcome Following Blood Pressure Drops Within 24 Hours After Successful Mechanical Thrombectomy. *Neurology*. [41886712](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41886712/)

**Research Question:** Do systolic blood pressure drops of ≥40 mmHg within 24 hours after successful mechanical thrombectomy worsen functional outcomes at 3 months?

Postprocedural BP drops occurred in 41% of patients after successful thrombectomy and were independently associated with worse 3-month functional outcomes (adjusted OR 1.66) and more than double the rate of early neurologic deterioration — making post-MT hemodynamic stability a modifiable target worth active attention.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective observational multicenter study, N=300, acute ischemic stroke patients with successful recanalization (mTICI ≥2b) from 2 German tertiary stroke centers, 2020–2022
- Primary outcome: mRS shift at 3 months — BP drops associated with worse disability (adjusted common OR 1.66, 95% CI 1.02–2.70); each additional drop carried incremental risk (adjusted common OR per +1 drop: 1.33, 95% CI 1.05–1.69); early neurologic deterioration 22.2% vs 13.4% (adjusted OR 2.22, 95% CI 1.03–4.78)
- Limitations: Retrospective design with inherent confounding; BP drops may be a marker of underlying instability (cardiac, autonomic, sedation-related) rather than an independent cause, limiting causal inference

Apixaban reduced ischemic stroke in ARTESIA's high-risk subgroup but NOAH-AFNET 6 showed no significant stroke reduction alongside substantial bleeding increases — leaving clinicians without a clear answer on who to anticoagulate.

**Imaging**

Comparative Accuracy of TCD, TTE, TEE, and Cardiac CT in Detecting Right-to-Left Shunt in Embolic Stroke of Undetermined Source. *Neurology: Clinical Practice*. [41908736](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41908736/)

**Research Question:** Which imaging modality — TCD, TTE, TEE, or cardiac CT — best detects right-to-left shunt in patients with embolic stroke of undetermined source?

TCD offers 100% sensitivity and NPV for RLS detection in ESUS, making it the optimal noninvasive first-line screen, while cardiac CT's perfect specificity but 45.8% sensitivity limits its standalone use.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective comparative study, N=130 (from 469 screened), ESUS patients who underwent ≥3 of 4 modalities at a single neurosonology center
- Primary outcome: TCD sensitivity 100%, specificity 75%, accuracy 87.6%; TEE sensitivity 95.1%, specificity 87.5%, accuracy 91.3% (highest overall); TTE sensitivity 83.3%, specificity 93.3%, accuracy 88.1%; cardiac CT sensitivity 45.8%, specificity 100%; no significant AUC difference among TCD, TTE, and TEE, but all three outperformed cardiac CT (p<0.05)
- Limitations: Class IV evidence only; single-center retrospective design with referral and selection bias, and PFO confirmation defined by concordance of ≥2 tests rather than a true independent gold standard

**Imaging**

Automated Intracranial Hemorrhage Detection: Real-World Experience in a Large Comprehensive Stroke Center. *Neurology: Clinical Practice*. [41908735](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41908735/)

**Research Question:** How does automated ICH detection software perform against board-certified radiologists in real-world code stroke patients?

Automated ICH detection achieved 81% overall sensitivity and 99% specificity, but sensitivity for isolated extra-axial hemorrhage was only 43% — a critical blind spot clinicians must account for when relying on these tools.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective cohort, N = 1,402 noncontrast CT head scans, consecutive code stroke patients at a single comprehensive stroke center (Jan 2022–Feb 2023)
- Primary outcome: Sensitivity 81% (95% CI 74%–88%), specificity 99% (98%–99%), PPV 85%, NPV 98%; sensitivity was 94% for intra-axial hemorrhage but only 43% for isolated extra-axial hemorrhage
- Limitations: Single-center retrospective design limits generalizability, and radiologist reports as gold standard introduce potential reference bias

**Rehabilitation**

Effects of Neuromuscular Electrical Stimulation on Swallowing Function for Post-Stroke Dysphagia: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis. *International Journal of Language & Communication Disorders*. [41888972](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41888972/)

**Research Question:** Does adding neuromuscular electrical stimulation (NMES) to conventional swallowing therapy improve dysphagia outcomes in post-stroke patients compared to therapy without NMES?

NMES combined with conventional swallowing therapy significantly reduced aspiration risk (Penetration Aspiration Scale MD −1.05, 95% CI −1.50 to −0.60) and improved functional swallowing scores, supporting its integration into post-stroke dysphagia rehabilitation.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 16 RCTs; population: post-stroke patients with dysphagia
- Primary outcome: Penetration Aspiration Scale MD −1.05 (95% CI −1.50 to −0.60, P < 0.00001); Functional Dysphagia Scale MD −7.23 (95% CI −13.62 to −0.84, P = 0.03); Functional Oral Intake Scale MD 0.66 (95% CI −0.31 to 1.62, P = 0.18, non-significant)
- Limitations: Small number of included studies with heterogeneous stimulation parameters and limited evidence that NMES alone outperforms conventional swallowing therapy

No-reflow phenomenon after vessel recanalization in acute ischemic stroke: mechanisms, diagnosis, and treatment directions. *Journal of Neurology*. [41915244](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41915244/)

**Research Question:** What are the mechanisms, diagnostic approaches, and treatment strategies for the no-reflow phenomenon in acute ischemic stroke patients who achieve successful large-vessel recanalization?

The no-reflow phenomenon affects 3.3–63% of patients after reperfusion therapy and worsens outcomes despite successful recanalization, but emerging strategies targeting pericytes, microthrombi, and neurovascular unit dysfunction offer potential therapeutic directions.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review; population: AIS patients undergoing reperfusion therapy
- Primary outcome: Prevalence of no-reflow phenomenon ranges from 3.3% to 63% post-recanalization; associated with significantly increased risk of poor clinical outcomes
- Limitations: As a narrative review, it does not provide pooled effect estimates or systematic evidence grading; the wide prevalence range reflects heterogeneous diagnostic criteria across studies, limiting clinical benchmarking
