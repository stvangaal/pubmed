**Prevention**

Left Atrial Appendage Closure or Anticoagulation for Atrial Fibrillation. *The New England Journal of Medicine*. [41910347](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910347/)

**Research Question:** In atrial fibrillation patients who are eligible for anticoagulation, is left atrial appendage closure (LAAC) noninferior to NOAC therapy for preventing cardiovascular death, stroke, or systemic embolism while reducing bleeding?

LAAC was noninferior to NOACs for the composite of cardiovascular death, stroke, or systemic embolism and cut non-procedure-related bleeding nearly in half — offering a compelling alternative even in anticoagulation-eligible AF patients.

**Details:**
- Design: Prospective, international, randomized trial (CHAMPION-AF); N=3000; AF patients eligible for anticoagulation, mean CHA₂DS₂-VASc 3.5
- Primary outcome: Efficacy — cardiovascular death/stroke/systemic embolism at 3 years: 5.7% (LAAC) vs. 4.8% (NOAC); difference 0.9 percentage points (95% CI −0.8 to 2.6), P<0.001 for noninferiority. Safety — non-procedure-related bleeding: 10.9% vs. 19.0% (HR 0.55; 95% CI 0.45–0.67), P<0.001 for superiority
- Limitations: Trial is ongoing and 3-year follow-up may not capture late device-related complications (e.g., device-related thrombus, late leaks); patients required short-term post-procedure anticoagulation, potentially underestimating real-world bleeding differences

**Prevention**

Intensive LDL Cholesterol Targeting in Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease. *New England Journal of Medicine*. [41910315](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910315/)

**Research Question:** In patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, does targeting LDL <55 mg/dL reduce cardiovascular events compared with targeting LDL <70 mg/dL?

Targeting LDL <55 mg/dL reduced the 3-year composite cardiovascular endpoint by a third compared with <70 mg/dL (6.6% vs 9.7%, HR 0.67), providing the first randomized trial evidence that the more aggressive secondary-prevention target is superior.

**Details:**
- Design: Open-label RCT, N=3,048, patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease in South Korea
- Primary outcome: Composite of CV death, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, revascularization, or hospitalization for unstable angina — 6.6% vs 9.7% (HR 0.67, 95% CI 0.52–0.86, P=0.002)
- Limitations: Open-label design with no placebo control; single-country cohort (South Korea) limits generalizability; achieved LDL separation was modest (56 vs 66 mg/dL median), meaning the true benefit of hitting guideline targets may be larger than demonstrated

**Prevention**

Antithrombotic therapy in patients with atrial fibrillation: unresolved issues and future directions. *Journal of Thrombosis and Haemostasis*. [41638577](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41638577/)

**Research Question:** Where do current anticoagulation strategies for AF fall short, and what emerging approaches may address residual stroke risk and bleeding burden?

Current AF anticoagulation leaves meaningful residual stroke risk and unresolved net-benefit questions in key subgroups—factor XI inhibitors, LAA closure, and implantable carotid filters represent the most promising horizon for safer, more effective prevention.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review; no trial-level N
- Primary outcome: Identifies five major gap areas: (1) limitations of CHA₂DS₂-VASc and bleeding scores; (2) populations where VKAs remain preferred (mechanical valves, antiphospholipid syndrome, rheumatic mitral stenosis); (3) uncertain net benefit in advanced CKD and post-intracranial hemorrhage survivors; (4) management of device- and wearable-detected AF and post-ablation anticoagulation; (5) residual and breakthrough stroke risk with ongoing anticoagulation
- Limitations: Narrative rather than systematic review; conclusions reflect author synthesis and do not derive from pooled quantitative data; no formal assessment of evidence quality

**Acute Treatment**

Early Versus Delayed Anticoagulation in Acute Ischemic Stroke According to Atrial Fibrillation Subtype and Time of Diagnosis: Subgroup Analysis of the OPTIMAS Randomized Controlled Trial. *Stroke*. [41919368](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41919368/)

**Research Question:** Does AF subtype (paroxysmal vs. persistent) or timing of AF diagnosis (before vs. after stroke) modify the treatment effect of early versus delayed DOAC initiation after acute ischemic stroke?

Neither AF subtype nor timing of AF diagnosis changed the effect of early versus delayed DOAC initiation, supporting a uniform approach to anticoagulation timing regardless of these AF characteristics — though persistent AF independently carried roughly double the risk of recurrent events compared with paroxysmal AF.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified subgroup analysis of OPTIMAS, a randomized open-label trial with blinded outcome assessment; N=3,619; mean age 78.0 years, 45.3% female
- Primary outcome: Composite of recurrent ischemic stroke, symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage, and systemic embolism; no interaction by AF diagnosis timing (Pinteraction=0.312) or AF subtype (Pinteraction=0.377); persistent AF independently associated with primary outcome (aOR 2.10, 95% CI 1.19–3.68) vs. paroxysmal AF
- Limitations: Subgroup analysis with limited statistical power to detect meaningful interactions; open-label design with potential for ascertainment bias in outcome assessment

**Acute Treatment**

Early vs Delayed Extubation After Thrombectomy for Acute Ischemic Stroke: The EDESTROKE Randomized Clinical Trial. *JAMA Neurology*. [41910960](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910960/)

**Research Question:** Does early extubation (<6 hours) compared with delayed extubation (6–12 hours) after successful thrombectomy under general anesthesia improve 90-day functional outcomes in acute ischemic stroke?

Early extubation after thrombectomy did not improve functional independence at 90 days compared with delayed extubation, suggesting clinicians have flexibility in extubation timing without compromising outcomes.

**Details:**
- Design: Single-center randomized clinical trial, N=174, adults with anterior circulation large-vessel occlusion stroke undergoing successful thrombectomy under general anesthesia
- Primary outcome: Functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days — 47.7% early vs 45.9% delayed (RR 1.04; 95% CI 0.76–1.43)
- Limitations: Single tertiary center limits generalizability, and the trial was likely underpowered to detect modest differences or establish true equivalence given the wide confidence intervals

**Acute Treatment**

Cerebral Edema in Patients With Large Ischemic Core After Thrombectomy: A Secondary Analysis of SELECT2 Randomized Trial. *Stroke*. [41906875](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41906875/)

**Research Question:** Does endovascular thrombectomy increase cerebral edema in patients with large ischemic cores, and does edema attenuate the functional benefit of thrombectomy?

Despite its overall clinical benefit, EVT in large-core stroke was independently associated with greater midline shift (6.39 mm vs 4.18 mm; aOR 1.63), which mediated approximately 11% of the reduction in EVT's effect on functional outcome — underscoring the need for proactive edema management in this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified secondary analysis of SELECT2 RCT; N=342 (after excluding 10 hemicraniectomy patients); adults with anterior circulation large vessel occlusion and large ischemic core (ASPECTS 3–5 or core ≥50 mL)
- Primary outcome: Maximum midline shift within 7 days — median 6.39 mm (IQR 0–12.0) with EVT vs 4.18 mm (IQR 0–9.66) with medical management (P=0.021); EVT independently associated with greater MLS (aOR 1.63, 95% CI 1.25–2.12); each mm of MLS associated with early neurological worsening (aOR 1.15) and worse 90-day mRS (aOR 0.96)
- Limitations: Secondary analysis with inherent selection bias; hemicraniectomy patients excluded, potentially removing the most severe edema cases and underestimating the true effect

**Prevention**

Evolocumab to Reduce First Major Cardiovascular Events in Patients Without Known Significant Atherosclerosis and With Diabetes: Results From the VESALIUS-CV Trial. *JAMA*. [41903215](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41903215/)

**Research Question:** Can evolocumab reduce first major cardiovascular events — including ischemic stroke — in patients with diabetes but without established significant atherosclerosis?

In diabetic patients with no prior MI or stroke and no known significant atherosclerosis, adding evolocumab to statin therapy reduced 3-P MACE (including ischemic stroke) by 31% over ~5 years, with a 2.1% absolute risk reduction — supporting expansion of PCSK9 inhibitor use beyond established atherosclerotic disease.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified subgroup analysis of a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial; N=3,655 (evolocumab 1,849 vs. placebo 1,806); median age 65 years, 57% female, all with diabetes and no prior revascularization, arterial stenosis ≥50%, or CAC ≥100
- Primary outcome: 3-P MACE (CHD death, MI, ischemic stroke) occurred in 5.0% vs. 7.1% (HR 0.69, 95% CI 0.52–0.91; P=.009); 4-P MACE 7.6% vs. 10.5% (HR 0.69, 95% CI 0.55–0.86; P=.001); all-cause mortality 7.8% vs. 10.1% (HR 0.76, 95% CI 0.61–0.95)
- Limitations: This is a subgroup analysis, not the primary trial result, which limits the strength of causal inference; the subgroup was entirely composed of diabetic patients, restricting generalizability to non-diabetic primary prevention populations

**Imaging**

Ischemic core reversal on neuroimaging following endovascular reperfusion: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Journal of Cerebral Blood Flow and Metabolism*. [41542894](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41542894/)

**Research Question:** How often does the presumed ischemic core reverse on follow-up imaging after endovascular thrombectomy, and what factors predict reversal?

Ischemic core reversal occurred in roughly 1 in 6 patients after EVT and was strongly associated with successful recanalization and better functional outcomes, suggesting early imaging regularly overestimates final infarct volume.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis, 14 studies, N = 3,640 patients with baseline and follow-up neuroimaging after EVT
- Primary outcome: Core reversal prevalence 17.5% (95% CI: 11.9%–25.1%); associated with successful recanalization (TICI 2B-3: OR 2.89, 95% CI: 1.65–5.06), IV thrombolysis (OR 1.41, 95% CI: 1.09–1.83), shorter onset-to-imaging time (median difference 90 min), and good functional outcome (mRS 0–2: OR 2.11, 95% CI: 1.55–2.86); diabetes was negatively associated (OR 0.64, 95% CI: 0.47–0.86)
- Limitations: High heterogeneity across studies with inconsistent imaging modalities (MRI vs. CT) and variable follow-up timing (24–168 h), making pooled prevalence estimates difficult to apply uniformly at the bedside

Effectiveness and safety of endovascular therapy compared to intravenous thrombolysis in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusions: a real-world multicentre study from the Italian SITS registry. *European Stroke Journal*. [41904677](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41904677/)

**Research Question:** Does endovascular therapy improve functional outcomes compared to intravenous thrombolysis alone in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusion in routine clinical practice?

EVT offered no functional benefit over IVT alone in MeVO stroke (mRS 0–2 at 90 days: 60.6% vs 60.9%) but was associated with significantly higher rates of intracranial haemorrhage, reinforcing a cautious approach to routine EVT in this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective multicentre registry study (Italian SITS registry, 82 centres, 2020–2023); propensity score–matched cohort, N = 780 (390 per group)
- Primary outcome: Functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days — 60.6% EVT ± IVT vs 60.9% IVT alone (OR 0.99, 95% CI 0.73–1.34, P = .939)
- Limitations: Retrospective registry design with incomplete procedural data and anatomical heterogeneity in MeVO classification limits causal inference and generalisability

**Acute Treatment**

Functional Outcome Following Blood Pressure Drops Within 24 Hours After Successful Mechanical Thrombectomy. *Neurology*. [41886712](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41886712/)

**Research Question:** Do systolic blood pressure drops of ≥40 mmHg within 24 hours after successful mechanical thrombectomy independently worsen functional outcomes at 3 months?

Systolic BP drops ≥40 mmHg within the first 24 hours after successful thrombectomy occurred in 41% of patients and were independently associated with worse 3-month disability (adjusted OR 1.66) and more than doubled the odds of early neurologic deterioration — making post-procedural BP stability a modifiable target worth actively managing on the stroke unit.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective observational multicenter study, N=300, patients with successful recanalization (mTICI ≥2b) from 2 German tertiary stroke centers, October 2020–September 2022
- Primary outcome: mRS shift analysis at 3 months — BP drops vs. no drops: adjusted OR 1.66 (95% CI 1.02–2.70); per additional drop: adjusted OR 1.33 (95% CI 1.05–1.69); early neurologic deterioration 22.2% vs. 13.4% (adjusted OR 2.22, 95% CI 1.03–4.78)
- Limitations: Retrospective design with inherent confounding; BP drop threshold (≥40 mmHg within 1 hour) is arbitrary and not validated; causes of BP drops (e.g., sedation, cardiac events) were not fully characterized, limiting causal inference

Nerinetide added to EVT without thrombolysis did not improve functional independence, mortality, or safety outcomes versus placebo, reinforcing the persistent failure of neuroprotective agents to translate from bench to bedside.

Efficacy and Safety of DOACs in Patients with Atrial Fibrillation and History of Falls or Risk of Falls: The Liverpool AF-Falls Project. A Systematic Review and Bayesian Network Meta-analysis. *Drugs & Aging*. [41845167](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41845167/)

**Research Question:** Which DOAC is most effective and safest for AF patients with a history of or risk for falls, a group often denied anticoagulation?

All DOACs outperformed VKAs for stroke prevention and major bleeding in fall-risk AF patients, with apixaban ranking highest for stroke/systemic embolism reduction (HR 0.72) and edoxaban ranking highest for major bleeding reduction (HR 0.66), supporting anticoagulation in this undertreated population.

**Details:**
- Design: Bayesian network meta-analysis of 10 studies (5 RCTs, 5 observational), fall-risk or falls-history AF patients
- Primary outcome: Stroke/systemic embolism — apixaban HR 0.72 (95% CrI 0.59–0.96), rivaroxaban HR 0.80 (0.63–0.99) vs. VKA; major bleeding — edoxaban HR 0.66 (0.50–0.92), apixaban HR 0.67 (0.55–0.87) vs. VKA
- Limitations: Moderate-to-serious risk of bias across included studies and small evidence base (only 10 articles), limiting confidence in the DOAC hierarchy

**Research Question:** Does clazosentan improve functional outcomes after subarachnoid hemorrhage across different patient subgroups, including the elderly and those with severe neurological grade?

Clazosentan independently doubled the odds of good functional outcome at 90 days and halved angiographic vasospasm rates in a real-world SAH cohort, with benefit observed regardless of age, sex, severity, or clot volume.

Persistent microvascular failure after successful recanalization affects 3.3–63% of AIS patients and worsens outcomes, yet targeted interventions including pericyte modulators and post-ischemic conditioning are emerging as potential treatment directions.

- Limitations: Narrative review without systematic methodology or meta-analysis; wide prevalence range reflects heterogeneous diagnostic criteria across studies, limiting generalizability of any single estimate
