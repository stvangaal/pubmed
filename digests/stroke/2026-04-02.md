Left Atrial Appendage Closure or Anticoagulation for Atrial Fibrillation. *The New England Journal of Medicine*. [41910347](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910347/)

**Research Question:** In patients with atrial fibrillation who are eligible for anticoagulation, is left atrial appendage closure (LAAC) noninferior to NOACs for preventing cardiovascular death, stroke, or systemic embolism while reducing bleeding?

LAAC was noninferior to NOACs for the composite efficacy endpoint and cut non-procedure-related bleeding nearly in half — findings that could support offering LAAC as a mainstream alternative to lifelong anticoagulation in AF patients.

**Details:**
- Design: Prospective, international, randomized trial (CHAMPION-AF); N=3,000; AF patients eligible for anticoagulation; mean CHA₂DS₂-VASc 3.5
- Primary outcome: Efficacy — cardiovascular death, stroke, or systemic embolism at 3 years: 5.7% (device) vs. 4.8% (NOAC); difference 0.9 percentage points (95% CI −0.8 to 2.6), P<0.001 for noninferiority. Safety — non-procedure-related bleeding: 10.9% vs. 19.0%; HR 0.55 (95% CI 0.45–0.67), P<0.001 for superiority
- Limitations: Trial is ongoing (3-year interim analysis), funded by Boston Scientific (device manufacturer), and procedure-related complications from LAAC implantation are not captured in the primary safety endpoint, potentially flattering the device arm's bleeding profile

**Prevention**

Intensive LDL Cholesterol Targeting in Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease. *New England Journal of Medicine*. [41910315](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910315/)

**Research Question:** In patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, does targeting LDL <55 mg/dL reduce cardiovascular events compared with targeting LDL <70 mg/dL?

Targeting LDL <55 mg/dL reduced the 3-year composite cardiovascular outcome by one-third compared with <70 mg/dL (6.6% vs. 9.7%; HR 0.67), providing the first randomized trial evidence to directly support the more intensive secondary prevention target now embedded in major guidelines.

**Details:**
- Design: Open-label randomized superiority trial, N=3048, patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease in South Korea
- Primary outcome: Composite of cardiovascular death, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, revascularization, or hospitalization for unstable angina — 6.6% vs. 9.7% (HR 0.67; 95% CI 0.52–0.86; P=0.002)
- Limitations: Open-label design introduces potential for bias; conducted in a single country (South Korea), limiting generalizability; median achieved LDL was 56 vs. 66 mg/dL, meaning separation between arms was modest (~10 mg/dL), and real-world adherence to intensive targets may be lower

**Prevention**

Antithrombotic therapy in patients with atrial fibrillation: unresolved issues and future directions. *Journal of Thrombosis and Haemostasis*. [41638577](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41638577/)

**Research Question:** What are the current gaps, unresolved clinical dilemmas, and emerging strategies in antithrombotic therapy for stroke prevention in atrial fibrillation?

Despite DOACs offering equivalent stroke protection with less intracranial bleeding than warfarin, substantial residual stroke risk persists on guideline-recommended anticoagulation, driving trials of intensified and combined strategies — including LAA closure, implantable carotid filters, and factor XI inhibitors — to improve the safety-efficacy balance.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review; no trial-level N; synthesizes existing evidence across multiple AF subpopulations and trial programs
- Primary outcome: Identifies key unresolved issues — uncertain net benefit of anticoagulation in advanced CKD and post-intracranial hemorrhage survivors; preferred VKA use in mechanical heart valves, antiphospholipid syndrome, and rheumatic mitral stenosis; management of device- and wearable-detected AF; and anticoagulation after AF ablation
- Limitations: Narrative (not systematic) review with no quantitative synthesis; conclusions reflect expert interpretation rather than pooled effect estimates

**Acute Treatment**

Early Versus Delayed Anticoagulation in Acute Ischemic Stroke According to Atrial Fibrillation Subtype and Time of Diagnosis: Subgroup Analysis of the OPTIMAS Randomized Controlled Trial. *Stroke*. [41919368](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41919368/)

**Research Question:** Does AF subtype (paroxysmal vs. persistent) or timing of AF diagnosis (before vs. after stroke) modify the treatment effect of early versus delayed DOAC initiation after acute ischemic stroke?

Neither AF subtype nor timing of AF diagnosis changed the benefit or risk of early DOAC initiation, supporting a uniform early-DOAC approach regardless of these clinical variables — though persistent AF independently carried roughly double the risk of recurrent ischemic stroke, intracranial hemorrhage, or systemic embolism compared with paroxysmal AF.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified subgroup analysis of OPTIMAS, a randomized open-label trial with blinded outcome assessment; N=3,619; mean age 78.0±9.9 years, 45.3% female, all with acute ischemic stroke and AF
- Primary outcome: Composite of recurrent ischemic stroke, symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage, and systemic embolism; no interaction by AF diagnosis timing (P=0.312) or subtype (P=0.377); persistent AF independently associated with increased primary outcome risk (aOR 2.10, 95% CI 1.19–3.68)
- Limitations: Subgroup analysis with limited statistical power to detect interaction effects; open-label design with potential for bias in outcome ascertainment despite blinded assessment

**Acute Treatment**

Early vs Delayed Extubation After Thrombectomy for Acute Ischemic Stroke: The EDESTROKE Randomized Clinical Trial. *JAMA Neurology*. [41910960](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910960/)

**Research Question:** Does early extubation (<6 hours) compared with delayed extubation (6–12 hours) after successful thrombectomy under general anesthesia improve 90-day functional outcomes in acute ischemic stroke?

Early extubation after thrombectomy did not improve functional independence at 90 days, suggesting clinicians can time extubation based on individual patient readiness rather than adhering to an early protocol.

**Details:**
- Design: Single-center randomized clinical trial, N=174, adults with anterior circulation large-vessel occlusion stroke undergoing successful thrombectomy under general anesthesia
- Primary outcome: Functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days — 47.7% early vs 45.9% delayed (RR 1.04; 95% CI 0.76–1.43)
- Limitations: Single tertiary center limits generalizability, and the trial was likely underpowered to detect modest differences in secondary safety outcomes such as pneumonia (21.8% vs 29.9%) and reintubation (4.6% vs 2.3%)

**Acute Treatment**

Cerebral Edema in Patients With Large Ischemic Core After Thrombectomy: A Secondary Analysis of SELECT2 Randomized Trial. *Stroke*. [41906875](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41906875/)

**Research Question:** Does endovascular thrombectomy increase cerebral edema in patients with large ischemic core, and does that edema affect clinical outcomes?

EVT in large-core stroke increases midline shift compared with medical management (6.39 vs 4.18 mm), and this edema mediates roughly 10% of EVT's functional benefit — meaning the net gain from thrombectomy is partially offset by edema-related harm, making proactive edema management a priority in this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified secondary analysis of SELECT2 RCT; N=342 (after excluding 10 hemicraniectomy patients); adults with anterior circulation LVO and ASPECTS 3–5 or core ≥50 mL
- Primary outcome: EVT independently associated with greater maximum midline shift within 7 days (aOR 1.63, 95% CI 1.25–2.12; P=0.0027); MLS associated with early neurological worsening (aOR 1.15 per mm, 95% CI 1.07–1.23) and worse 90-day mRS (aOR 0.96, 95% CI 0.93–0.98)
- Limitations: Secondary analysis with inherent risk of confounding; hemicraniectomy patients excluded, likely removing the most severe edema cases and underestimating the true magnitude of edema burden

**Prevention**

Evolocumab to Reduce First Major Cardiovascular Events in Patients Without Known Significant Atherosclerosis and With Diabetes: Results From the VESALIUS-CV Trial. *JAMA*. [41903215](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41903215/)

**Research Question:** Does evolocumab reduce first major cardiovascular events in high-risk diabetic patients without established significant atherosclerosis?

Evolocumab added to statin therapy reduced 3-P MACE by 31% (HR 0.69, 95% CI 0.52–0.91) and all-cause mortality by 24% (HR 0.76, 95% CI 0.61–0.95) in diabetic patients with no prior MI or stroke and no known significant atherosclerosis, supporting PCSK9 inhibition as a primary prevention strategy in this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified subgroup analysis of a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial; N=3,655 (evolocumab 1,849, placebo 1,806); high-risk diabetes patients without known significant atherosclerosis across 774 sites in 33 countries; median follow-up 4.8 years
- Primary outcome: 3-P MACE 5.0% vs 7.1% (HR 0.69, 95% CI 0.52–0.91, P=.009); 4-P MACE 7.6% vs 10.5% (HR 0.69, 95% CI 0.55–0.86, P=.001); median LDL-C 52 mg/dL vs 111 mg/dL at 48 weeks
- Limitations: This is a prespecified subgroup analysis rather than a standalone trial, limiting the strength of causal inference; all patients had diabetes, so findings cannot be generalised to non-diabetic primary prevention populations

**Acute Treatment**

Efficacy and safety of intravenous nerinetide in acute ischemic stroke patients undergoing endovascular thrombectomy without thrombolysis: a meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials. *Neuroradiology*. [41400652](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41400652/)

**Research Question:** Does intravenous nerinetide improve outcomes in acute ischemic stroke patients undergoing EVT who did not receive IV thrombolysis?

Nerinetide added to EVT conferred no benefit over placebo in the thrombolysis-naïve subgroup — 90-day functional independence (mRS 0–2 RR 1.07, p=0.35) and mortality were unchanged — reinforcing that this neuroprotective agent should not be adopted in routine EVT practice.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 3 RCTs; N=1,394 (726 nerinetide, 668 placebo), all EVT without IVT
- Primary outcome: 90-day mRS 0–2 (RR 1.07, 95% CI 0.93–1.22, p=0.35); mRS 0–1 (RR 1.02, 95% CI 0.71–1.47, p=0.92)
- Limitations: Only 3 RCTs were available, and the analysis is restricted to a subgroup (no-IVT patients) rather than pre-specified primary trial populations, limiting statistical power and generalizability

**Imaging**

Ischemic core reversal on neuroimaging following endovascular reperfusion: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Journal of Cerebral Blood Flow and Metabolism*. [41542894](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41542894/)

**Research Question:** How often does tissue classified as ischemic core on baseline imaging recover after endovascular thrombectomy, and what factors predict this reversal?

Ischemic core reversal occurred in nearly 1 in 5 patients after EVT and was associated with more than twice the odds of good functional outcome, challenging the assumption that baseline imaging-defined core is irreversibly infarcted.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis, 14 studies, N = 3,640 patients with AIS undergoing EVT
- Primary outcome: Pooled core reversal prevalence 17.5% (95% CI: 11.9%–25.1%); reversal associated with mRS 0–2 (OR 2.11, 95% CI: 1.55–2.86)
- Limitations: Substantial heterogeneity across studies in imaging modality, timing of follow-up, and definition of core reversal limits direct comparisons and pooled estimates

**Acute Treatment**

Effectiveness and safety of endovascular therapy compared to intravenous thrombolysis in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusions: a real-world multicentre study from the Italian SITS registry. *European Stroke Journal*. [41904677](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41904677/)

**Research Question:** Does endovascular therapy improve functional outcomes compared to intravenous thrombolysis alone in real-world patients with medium-vessel occlusion stroke?

EVT provided no functional benefit over IVT alone in MeVO stroke (mRS 0–2 at 90 days: 60.6% vs 60.9%; OR 0.99) but increased overall haemorrhagic complications (18.4% vs 11.2%), reinforcing a cautious approach to routine EVT in this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective multicentre registry study (SITS, 82 Italian centres, 2020–2023); propensity score–matched cohort, N = 780 (390 per group)
- Primary outcome: Functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days — 60.6% EVT ± IVT vs 60.9% IVT alone (OR 0.99; 95% CI 0.73–1.34; P = .939)
- Limitations: Retrospective registry design with incomplete procedural data and anatomical heterogeneity in MeVO classification limits causal inference and generalisability

**Hospital Care**

Functional Outcome Following Blood Pressure Drops Within 24 Hours After Successful Mechanical Thrombectomy. *Neurology*. [41886712](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41886712/)

**Research Question:** Do systolic blood pressure drops of ≥40 mmHg within 24 hours after successful mechanical thrombectomy worsen functional outcomes at 3 months?

Post-thrombectomy BP drops occurred in 41% of patients and were independently associated with worse 3-month disability (adjusted OR 1.66) and more than double the rate of early neurologic deterioration, making postprocedural BP stability a potentially modifiable target for improving outcomes.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective multicenter observational study, N=300, adults with successful recanalization (mTICI ≥2b) from 2 German tertiary stroke centers, 2020–2022
- Primary outcome: mRS shift at 3 months — BP drops associated with worse functional outcome (any drop vs none: adjusted common OR 1.66, 95% CI 1.02–2.70; per additional drop: adjusted common OR 1.33, 95% CI 1.05–1.69); early neurologic deterioration 22.2% vs 13.4% (adjusted OR 2.22, 95% CI 1.03–4.78)
- Limitations: Retrospective design precludes causal inference; BP drops may be a marker of underlying instability or comorbidity rather than an independent driver of harm, and unmeasured confounding cannot be excluded

**Research Question:** Does clazosentan improve functional outcomes after subarachnoid hemorrhage across clinically relevant patient subgroups, including older patients and those with severe neurological grade?

Clazosentan was independently associated with favorable 90-day functional outcome (mRS 0–2) in SAH patients across all subgroups examined, with an OR of 2.17, suggesting benefit that extends beyond the populations studied in registration trials.

**Imaging**

Comparative Accuracy of TCD, TTE, TEE, and Cardiac CT in Detecting Right-to-Left Shunt in Embolic Stroke of Undetermined Source. *Neurology: Clinical Practice*. [41908736](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41908736/)

**Research Question:** In patients with embolic stroke of undetermined source (ESUS), which imaging modality — TCD, TTE, TEE, or cardiac CT — best detects right-to-left shunt due to PFO?

TCD achieved 100% sensitivity and NPV for RLS detection, making it the strongest rule-out tool, while TEE had the highest overall accuracy (91.3%); cardiac CT's perfect specificity was undermined by only 45.8% sensitivity, limiting its standalone use.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective comparative study, N=130, ESUS patients who underwent ≥3 of 4 modalities at a single neurosonology center
- Primary outcome: TCD sensitivity 100%, specificity 75%, accuracy 87.6%; TEE sensitivity 95.1%, specificity 87.5%, accuracy 91.3%; TTE sensitivity 83.3%, specificity 93.3%, accuracy 88.1%; cardiac CT sensitivity 45.8%, specificity 100%; no significant AUC difference among TCD, TTE, and TEE, but all three outperformed cardiac CT (p<0.05)
- Limitations: Class IV evidence only; single-center retrospective design with referral and selection bias; PFO confirmation defined by concordance of ≥2 tests rather than a true independent gold standard
