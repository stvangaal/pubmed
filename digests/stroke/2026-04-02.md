**Prevention**

Left Atrial Appendage Closure or Anticoagulation for Atrial Fibrillation. *The New England Journal of Medicine*. [41910347](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910347/)

**Research Question:** In AF patients who are suitable anticoagulation candidates, is left atrial appendage closure (LAAC) noninferior to NOACs for preventing cardiovascular death, stroke, or systemic embolism while reducing bleeding?

LAAC was noninferior to NOACs for the composite of cardiovascular death, stroke, or systemic embolism (5.7% vs 4.8% at 3 years) and cut non-procedure-related bleeding nearly in half (10.9% vs 19.0%), supporting LAAC as a legitimate alternative to lifelong anticoagulation in eligible AF patients.

**Details:**
- Design: Prospective, international, randomized controlled trial (CHAMPION-AF); N=3000; AF patients eligible for anticoagulation, mean CHA₂DS₂-VASc 3.5
- Primary outcome: Efficacy — cardiovascular death/stroke/systemic embolism 5.7% (LAAC) vs 4.8% (NOAC), difference 0.9 percentage points (95% CI −0.8 to 2.6), p<0.001 for noninferiority; Safety — non-procedure-related bleeding HR 0.55 (95% CI 0.45–0.67), p<0.001 for superiority
- Limitations: Trial is ongoing and 3-year follow-up may not capture long-term device-related complications (e.g., device-related thrombus, late leaks); procedure-related adverse events are excluded from the bleeding comparison, potentially flattering the LAAC arm

**Prevention**

Intensive LDL Cholesterol Targeting in Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease. *New England Journal of Medicine*. [41910315](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910315/)

**Research Question:** In patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, does targeting LDL <55 mg/dL reduce cardiovascular events compared with targeting LDL <70 mg/dL?

Targeting LDL <55 mg/dL reduced the 3-year composite of cardiovascular death, MI, stroke, revascularization, or unstable angina hospitalization by one-third compared with <70 mg/dL (6.6% vs. 9.7%; HR 0.67), with no meaningful difference in safety outcomes — providing the first randomized trial evidence that the more intensive guideline target is superior.

**Details:**
- Design: Open-label randomized superiority trial, N=3,048, patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease in South Korea
- Primary outcome: Composite cardiovascular event rate 6.6% (intensive, LDL target <55 mg/dL) vs. 9.7% (conventional, LDL target <70 mg/dL); HR 0.67 (95% CI 0.52–0.86; P=0.002) at 3 years
- Limitations: Open-label design introduces potential for performance bias; median achieved LDL separation was only 10 mg/dL (56 vs. 66 mg/dL), limiting conclusions about the target thresholds themselves versus the degree of LDL lowering; single-country population may limit generalizability

**Prevention**

Antithrombotic therapy in patients with atrial fibrillation: unresolved issues and future directions. *Journal of Thrombosis and Haemostasis*. [41638577](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41638577/)

**Research Question:** What are the unresolved clinical challenges and emerging strategies in antithrombotic therapy for stroke prevention in atrial fibrillation?

Current anticoagulation leaves a substantial residual stroke risk in AF patients, and several high-stakes subgroups—including those with advanced kidney disease, prior intracranial bleeding, device-detected AF, or post-ablation status—lack robust evidence to guide therapy, making individualized decision-making essential.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review; no primary trial data or N reported
- Primary outcome: Synthesis of evidence gaps across AF subpopulations, risk score limitations, emerging technologies (LAA closure, implantable carotid filters), and factor XI inhibitors as a safer anticoagulant class
- Limitations: As a narrative review without systematic methods or meta-analysis, it reflects expert synthesis rather than quantified evidence; conclusions on uncertain subgroups are opinion-informed rather than data-driven

**Acute Treatment**

Early Versus Delayed Anticoagulation in Acute Ischemic Stroke According to Atrial Fibrillation Subtype and Time of Diagnosis: Subgroup Analysis of the OPTIMAS Randomized Controlled Trial. *Stroke*. [41919368](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41919368/)

**Research Question:** Does AF subtype (paroxysmal vs. persistent) or timing of AF diagnosis (before vs. after stroke) modify the treatment effect of early versus delayed DOAC initiation after acute ischemic stroke?

Neither AF subtype nor timing of AF diagnosis changed the balance of benefit and risk between early and delayed DOAC initiation, meaning the OPTIMAS main trial result applies broadly — but persistent AF carried roughly double the risk of recurrent ischemic stroke, intracranial hemorrhage, or embolism compared with paroxysmal AF regardless of treatment timing.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified subgroup analysis of a randomized, open-label, blinded-outcome trial; N=3,619; mean age 78.0±9.9 years, 45.3% female, all with acute ischemic stroke and AF
- Primary outcome: Composite of recurrent ischemic stroke, symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage, and systemic embolism; no interaction by AF diagnosis timing (P=0.312) or subtype (P=0.377); persistent vs. paroxysmal AF independently associated with primary outcome (aOR 2.10, 95% CI 1.19–3.68)
- Limitations: Subgroup analysis with interaction p-values >0.30, indicating insufficient power to detect plausible effect modification; open-label design introduces potential ascertainment bias

**Acute Treatment**

Early vs Delayed Extubation After Thrombectomy for Acute Ischemic Stroke: The EDESTROKE Randomized Clinical Trial. *JAMA Neurology*. [41910960](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910960/)

**Research Question:** Does early extubation (<6 hours) compared with delayed extubation (6–12 hours) after successful thrombectomy under general anesthesia improve 90-day functional outcomes in acute ischemic stroke?

Early extubation after thrombectomy did not improve functional independence at 90 days, meaning clinicians can defer extubation timing decisions to clinical readiness rather than a strict early protocol.

**Details:**
- Design: Single-center randomized clinical trial, N=174, adults with anterior circulation large-vessel occlusion undergoing successful thrombectomy under general anesthesia
- Primary outcome: Functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days — 47.7% (early) vs 45.9% (delayed); RR 1.04 (95% CI 0.76–1.43)
- Limitations: Single tertiary center limits generalizability, and the trial was likely underpowered to detect modest differences in secondary safety outcomes such as pneumonia (21.8% vs 29.9%) and reintubation (4.6% vs 2.3%)

**Acute Treatment**

Cerebral Edema in Patients With Large Ischemic Core After Thrombectomy: A Secondary Analysis of SELECT2 Randomized Trial. *Stroke*. [41906875](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41906875/)

**Research Question:** Does endovascular thrombectomy increase cerebral edema in patients with large ischemic core, and does that edema attenuate the functional benefit of reperfusion?

EVT independently increased midline shift versus medical management in large-core stroke, and this edema mediated 10.6% of the attenuation of EVT's functional benefit — meaning successful reperfusion in these patients carries a real secondary injury risk that requires active monitoring and management.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified secondary analysis of the SELECT2 RCT; N=342 (after excluding 10 hemicraniectomy patients), adults with anterior circulation large vessel occlusion and large ischemic core (ASPECTS 3–5 or core ≥50 mL)
- Primary outcome: Median maximum midline shift within 7 days was 6.39 mm (IQR 0–12.0) with EVT vs. 4.18 mm (IQR 0–9.66) with medical management (P=0.021); EVT independently associated with greater midline shift (aOR 1.63, 95% CI 1.25–2.12); greater midline shift associated with early neurological worsening (aOR 1.15 per mm, 95% CI 1.07–1.23) and worse 90-day mRS (aOR 0.96, 95% CI 0.93–0.98)
- Limitations: Secondary analysis with inherent selection bias; hemicraniectomy patients excluded, likely removing the most severe edema cases and underestimating the true effect

**Prevention**

Evolocumab to Reduce First Major Cardiovascular Events in Patients Without Known Significant Atherosclerosis and With Diabetes: Results From the VESALIUS-CV Trial. *JAMA*. [41903215](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41903215/)

**Research Question:** Can the PCSK9 inhibitor evolocumab prevent a first major cardiovascular event in high-risk diabetic patients who lack known significant atherosclerosis?

Evolocumab reduced 3-P MACE by 31% (HR 0.69; 5-year absolute risk 5.0% vs 7.1%) in diabetic patients without prior MI, stroke, or significant atherosclerosis — supporting expansion of PCSK9 inhibitor use into primary prevention for this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified subgroup of a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial; N=3,655; diabetic patients without known significant atherosclerosis across 774 sites in 33 countries; median follow-up 4.8 years
- Primary outcome: 3-P MACE (CHD death, MI, ischemic stroke): HR 0.69 (95% CI 0.52–0.91), p=0.009; 4-P MACE: HR 0.69 (95% CI 0.55–0.86), p=0.001; all-cause mortality HR 0.76 (95% CI 0.61–0.95)
- Limitations: This is a prespecified subgroup analysis rather than an independently powered trial, limiting the strength of causal inference for this specific population

**Acute Treatment**

Efficacy and safety of intravenous nerinetide in acute ischemic stroke patients undergoing endovascular thrombectomy without thrombolysis: a meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials. *Neuroradiology*. [41400652](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41400652/)

**Research Question:** Does intravenous nerinetide improve outcomes in acute ischemic stroke patients undergoing EVT who do not receive IV thrombolysis?

Across 3 RCTs and 1,394 patients, nerinetide added to EVT without thrombolysis produced no significant improvement in functional independence, mortality, or safety outcomes — reinforcing the persistent failure of neuroprotective agents to translate from bench to bedside.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 3 RCTs; N = 1,394 (726 nerinetide, 668 placebo), all EVT without IVT
- Primary outcome: 90-day mRS 0–2 RR 1.07 (95% CI 0.93–1.22, P = 0.35); 90-day mRS 0–1 RR 1.02 (95% CI 0.71–1.47, P = 0.92)
- Limitations: Only 3 trials included, limiting power to detect modest treatment effects; pooling across trials with potentially heterogeneous patient populations and EVT protocols may obscure subgroup signals

**Imaging**

Ischemic core reversal on neuroimaging following endovascular reperfusion: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Journal of Cerebral Blood Flow and Metabolism*. [41542894](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41542894/)

**Research Question:** How often does the ischemic core reverse on follow-up imaging after endovascular thrombectomy, and what factors predict reversal?

Core reversal occurred in roughly 1 in 6 patients after EVT and was associated with more than double the odds of good functional outcome, suggesting that early imaging-defined infarct volumes may systematically overestimate true irreversible injury and should not be used in isolation to exclude patients from treatment.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis, 14 studies, N = 3,640 patients with baseline and follow-up imaging after EVT
- Primary outcome: Core reversal prevalence 17.5% (95% CI: 11.9%–25.1%); reversal associated with mRS 0–2 (OR 2.11, 95% CI: 1.55–2.86)
- Limitations: High heterogeneity across studies with varying imaging modalities (MR 19.9% vs. CT 15.9%) and follow-up time points (24–48 h vs. 48–168 h), limiting generalizability of the pooled prevalence estimate

**Prevention**

Efficacy and Safety of DOACs in Patients with Atrial Fibrillation and History of Falls or Risk of Falls: The Liverpool AF-Falls Project. A Systematic Review and Bayesian Network Meta-analysis. *Drugs & Aging*. [41845167](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41845167/)

**Research Question:** Which DOAC provides the best balance of stroke prevention and bleeding safety in AF patients with a history of or risk for falls?

Across 10 studies, apixaban ranked highest for stroke/systemic embolism reduction (HR 0.72 vs. VKA) while edoxaban ranked highest for major bleeding reduction (HR 0.66 vs. VKA), supporting DOAC use over VKAs even in fall-prone AF patients — with apixaban offering the most favorable overall profile.

**Details:**
- Design: Bayesian network meta-analysis of 5 RCTs and 5 observational studies; N not reported in aggregate; AF patients with history of or risk of falls
- Primary outcome: Stroke/systemic embolism — apixaban HR 0.72 (95% CrI 0.59–0.96, SUCRA 0.87), rivaroxaban HR 0.80 (95% CrI 0.63–0.99, SUCRA 0.68) vs. VKA; Major bleeding — edoxaban HR 0.66 (95% CrI 0.50–0.92, SUCRA 0.86), apixaban HR 0.67 (95% CrI 0.55–0.87, SUCRA 0.83) vs. VKA
- Limitations: Moderate-to-serious risk of bias across included studies limits confidence in the network estimates and head-to-head DOAC comparisons
