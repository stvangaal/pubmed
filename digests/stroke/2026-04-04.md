Left Atrial Appendage Closure or Anticoagulation for Atrial Fibrillation. *The New England Journal of Medicine*. [41910347](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910347/)

**Research Question:** In atrial fibrillation patients who are suitable candidates for anticoagulation, is device-based left atrial appendage closure (LAAC) noninferior to NOACs for preventing cardiovascular death, stroke, or systemic embolism?

LAAC met noninferiority for stroke and cardiovascular death compared to NOACs and cut major bleeding nearly in half, making it a credible alternative to lifelong anticoagulation in eligible AF patients.

**Details:**
- Design: Prospective, international, randomized trial (CHAMPION-AF); N=3,000; AF patients suitable for anticoagulation; mean CHA₂DS₂-VASc 3.5
- Primary outcome: Composite of cardiovascular death, stroke, or systemic embolism at 3 years — 5.7% (LAAC) vs. 4.8% (NOAC); difference 0.9 percentage points (95% CI −0.8 to 2.6), P<0.001 for noninferiority; non-procedure-related bleeding 10.9% vs. 19.0% (HR 0.55; 95% CI 0.45–0.67), P<0.001 for superiority
- Limitations: Trial is ongoing and 3-year follow-up may not capture late device-related complications or delayed thromboembolic events; industry-funded by Boston Scientific; noninferiority margin of 4.8 percentage points is relatively wide

**Prevention**

Left Atrial Appendage Closure or Medical Therapy in Atrial Fibrillation. *The New England Journal of Medicine*. [41849741](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41849741/)

**Research Question:** In atrial fibrillation patients at high risk for both stroke and bleeding, is left atrial appendage closure (LAAC) noninferior to physician-directed best medical therapy for preventing stroke, embolism, major bleeding, or cardiovascular death?

LAAC failed to demonstrate noninferiority to best medical care — including DOACs where eligible — in high-risk AF patients, with numerically more events in the device group (16.8 vs. 13.3 per 100 patient-years), meaning LAAC cannot be recommended as a routine alternative to optimized anticoagulation in this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Multicenter randomized equivalence trial, N=888 analyzed (446 device, 442 medical therapy), elderly AF patients in Germany (mean age 77.9 years, mean CHA₂DS₂-VASc 5.2, mean HAS-BLED 3.0)
- Primary outcome: Composite of stroke, systemic embolism, major bleeding, or cardiovascular/unexplained death — 155 events in device group vs. 127 in medical-therapy group (16.8 vs. 13.3 per 100 patient-years); noninferiority not met (P=0.44)
- Limitations: Conducted entirely in Germany, limiting generalizability; physician-directed "best medical care" may not reflect real-world anticoagulation prescribing, and a meaningful proportion of medical-therapy patients may not have received DOACs, potentially inflating the comparator's performance

**Prevention**

Intensive LDL Cholesterol Targeting in Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease. *New England Journal of Medicine*. [41910315](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910315/)

**Research Question:** In patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, does targeting LDL <55 mg/dL reduce cardiovascular events compared with targeting LDL <70 mg/dL?

Targeting LDL <55 mg/dL cut the 3-year rate of major cardiovascular events by one-third compared with the conventional <70 mg/dL target, with no increase in serious safety events — providing the first large RCT evidence directly supporting the more aggressive guideline threshold.

**Details:**
- Design: Open-label superiority RCT, N=3048, patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease in South Korea
- Primary outcome: Composite of CV death, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, revascularization, or hospitalization for unstable angina — 6.6% (intensive) vs. 9.7% (conventional); HR 0.67 (95% CI 0.52–0.86; P=0.002)
- Limitations: Open-label design risks ascertainment bias; median achieved LDL difference between arms was only 10 mg/dL (56 vs. 66 mg/dL), limiting the contrast; single-country population may limit generalizability

**Acute Treatment**

Current Management of Cerebral Venous Thrombosis. *Stroke*. [41924888](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41924888/)

**Research Question:** Across the full disease continuum of cerebral venous thrombosis, what constitutes current best practice for acute treatment, anticoagulation choice, secondary prevention, and long-term complication management?

Anticoagulation remains the cornerstone of CVT management even with intracranial hemorrhage, DOACs appear comparable to vitamin K antagonists for most patients, and long-term morbidity from fatigue, cognitive disturbance, and epilepsy remains substantial despite high rates of functional independence.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review (no trial data reported directly)
- Primary outcome: No single primary outcome; review covers acute anticoagulation, endovascular therapy, decompressive craniectomy for severe cases, DOAC vs. VKA comparisons, secondary prevention risk stratification (highest recurrence risk in cancer, antiphospholipid antibody syndrome, prior VTE, and idiopathic events), and chronic sequelae
- Limitations: Review article without meta-analytic pooling; evidence gaps explicitly acknowledged across all disease phases, particularly regarding optimal DOAC lead-in duration and total treatment length

**Acute Treatment**

Early Versus Delayed Anticoagulation in Acute Ischemic Stroke According to Atrial Fibrillation Subtype and Time of Diagnosis: Subgroup Analysis of the OPTIMAS Randomized Controlled Trial. *Stroke*. [41919368](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41919368/)

**Research Question:** Does AF subtype (paroxysmal vs. persistent) or timing of AF diagnosis (before vs. after stroke) modify the effect of early versus delayed DOAC initiation after acute ischemic stroke?

Neither AF subtype nor timing of AF diagnosis changed the treatment effect of early DOAC initiation, supporting a uniform approach to anticoagulation timing regardless of these AF characteristics — though persistent AF independently carried twice the risk of recurrent events compared with paroxysmal AF.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified subgroup analysis of OPTIMAS, a randomized open-label trial with blinded outcome assessment; N=3,619; mean age 78.0 years, 45.3% female
- Primary outcome: Composite of recurrent ischemic stroke, symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage, and systemic embolism; no significant interaction by AF diagnosis timing (P=0.312) or AF subtype (P=0.377); persistent AF independently associated with ~double the primary outcome risk vs. paroxysmal AF (aOR 2.10, 95% CI 1.19–3.68)
- Limitations: Subgroup analysis with interaction tests likely underpowered to detect modest effect modification; open-label design introduces potential for bias in management decisions

**Acute Treatment**

Early vs Delayed Extubation After Thrombectomy for Acute Ischemic Stroke: The EDESTROKE Randomized Clinical Trial. *JAMA Neurology*. [41910960](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910960/)

**Research Question:** Does early extubation (<6 hours) versus delayed extubation (6–12 hours) after successful thrombectomy under general anesthesia improve 90-day functional outcomes in acute ischemic stroke?

Early extubation after thrombectomy did not improve functional independence at 90 days compared with delayed extubation, suggesting clinicians have flexibility in extubation timing without compromising outcomes.

**Details:**
- Design: Single-center randomized clinical trial, N=174, adults with anterior circulation large-vessel occlusion stroke undergoing successful thrombectomy under general anesthesia
- Primary outcome: Functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days — 47.7% early vs 45.9% delayed (RR 1.04; 95% CI 0.76–1.43)
- Limitations: Single tertiary center limits generalizability, and the trial was likely underpowered to detect modest but clinically meaningful differences in secondary outcomes such as pneumonia (21.8% vs 29.9%) that trended in favor of early extubation

**Acute Treatment**

Cerebral Edema in Patients With Large Ischemic Core After Thrombectomy: A Secondary Analysis of SELECT2 Randomized Trial. *Stroke*. [41906875](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41906875/)

**Research Question:** Does endovascular thrombectomy increase cerebral edema in patients with large ischemic cores, and does that edema attenuate the functional benefit of thrombectomy?

EVT in large-core stroke produces significantly greater midline shift than medical management (6.39 vs 4.18 mm), and this edema partially offsets functional gains — mediating a 10.6% reduction in EVT's treatment effect at 90 days.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified secondary analysis of SELECT2 RCT; N=342 (after excluding 10 hemicraniectomy patients); large-core anterior circulation LVO (ASPECTS 3–5 or core ≥50 mL)
- Primary outcome: EVT independently associated with greater maximum midline shift within 7 days (adjusted OR 1.63, 95% CI 1.25–2.12; P=0.0027); MLS associated with early neurological worsening (aOR 1.15 per mm, 95% CI 1.07–1.23) and worse 90-day mRS (aOR 0.96, 95% CI 0.93–0.98)
- Limitations: Secondary analysis with imaging follow-up not standardized across sites; hemicraniectomy patients excluded, likely removing the most severe edema cases and underestimating the true effect

**Prevention**

Evolocumab to Reduce First Major Cardiovascular Events in Patients Without Known Significant Atherosclerosis and With Diabetes: Results From the VESALIUS-CV Trial. *JAMA*. [41903215](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41903215/)

**Research Question:** Can the PCSK9 inhibitor evolocumab prevent a first major cardiovascular event in high-risk diabetic patients who lack known significant atherosclerosis?

In diabetic patients without established atherosclerotic disease, adding evolocumab to statin therapy reduced 3-P MACE by 31% over ~5 years, suggesting PCSK9 inhibition has a meaningful primary prevention role in this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Prespecified subgroup analysis of a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial; N=3,655 (1,849 evolocumab, 1,806 placebo); patients with diabetes, LDL-C ≥90 mg/dL, no prior MI or stroke, and no known significant atherosclerosis; median follow-up 4.8 years
- Primary outcome: 3-P MACE (CHD death, MI, ischemic stroke) occurred in 5.0% vs 7.1% (HR 0.69, 95% CI 0.52–0.91; P=.009); 4-P MACE 7.6% vs 10.5% (HR 0.69, 95% CI 0.55–0.86; P=.001); all-cause mortality 7.8% vs 10.1% (HR 0.76, 95% CI 0.61–0.95)
- Limitations: This is a prespecified subgroup analysis rather than a standalone trial, limiting the strength of causal inference; the "no significant atherosclerosis" subgroup was defined partly by absence of coronary artery calcium scoring in many patients, introducing potential misclassification

Atrial fibrillation: an underappreciated complication of diabetes. *European Heart Journal*. [41668366](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41668366/)

**Research Question:** Should atrial fibrillation be formally recognized as a complication of diabetes, and how should it be managed across the spectrum of diabetic patients?

AF affects up to 25% of people with diabetes and can arise independently of hypertension, heart failure, or atherosclerotic disease — clinicians should screen for and manage AF as a direct diabetic complication, not merely a coincidental finding.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review; no trial N applicable; population is individuals with Type 2 diabetes at risk for or diagnosed with AF
- Primary outcome: AF prevalence up to 25% in diabetes, with rising incidence driven by expanding global diabetes burden; pathogenesis involves atrial structural, electrical, electromechanical, and autonomic remodelling plus metabolic comorbidities
- Limitations: Review article without systematic methodology or meta-analytic pooling; causal directionality and magnitude of independent diabetes–AF risk are not quantified from primary data

The efficacy and safety of intra-arterial tenecteplase after recanalization in ischemic stroke: A systematic review and meta-analysis with trial sequential analysis and GRADE assessment. *Journal of the Neurological Sciences*. [41793929](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41793929/)

**Research Question:** Does adjunctive intra-arterial tenecteplase (IA-TNK) after successful endovascular thrombectomy improve functional outcomes in acute ischemic stroke without increasing hemorrhagic risk?

Adding IA-TNK after successful EVT increased the rate of excellent functional outcome (mRS 0–1) by 8 percentage points (NNT 13) with no significant increase in symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage or mortality — a clinically meaningful signal for addressing futile recanalization.

**Details:**

- Limitations: Evidence certainty graded via GRADE (levels not specified in abstract); pooled analysis of a small number of trials with likely heterogeneous dosing strategies limits confidence in the point estimate

**Imaging**

Ischemic core reversal on neuroimaging following endovascular reperfusion: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Journal of Cerebral Blood Flow and Metabolism*. [41542894](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41542894/)

**Research Question:** How often does the ischemic core reverse on follow-up imaging after endovascular thrombectomy, and what factors predict reversal?

In 14 studies of 3,640 EVT patients, the ischemic core reversed in 17.5% of cases — meaning tissue initially classified as irreversibly infarcted was viable at follow-up — with reversal strongly tied to successful recanalization (OR 2.89) and associated with better functional outcomes (mRS 0–2: OR 2.11).

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis, N = 3,640, acute ischemic stroke patients undergoing EVT across 14 studies
- Primary outcome: Core reversal prevalence 17.5% (95% CI: 11.9%–25.1%); higher with MR-based imaging (19.9% vs. 15.9% CT-based) and at 24–48 h versus 48–168 h follow-up
- Limitations: High heterogeneity across studies in imaging modality, timing of follow-up, and core definition; observational designs preclude causal inference about predictors

Impact of atrial remodeling on efficacy of catheter ablation in reducing cardiovascular risk in atrial fibrillation: Results from CABANA trial. *Heart Rhythm*. [40250510](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40250510/)

**Research Question:** Does left atrial diameter modify the benefit of catheter ablation over drug therapy on major cardiovascular outcomes in atrial fibrillation?

In CABANA patients with non-enlarged left atria (≤40 mm), catheter ablation reduced the composite of death, disabling stroke, serious bleeding, or cardiac arrest by 70% (HR 0.30) and cut total mortality by 91% (HR 0.09), whereas no significant benefit was seen in those with enlarged left atria (>40 mm).

**Details:**
- Design: Post-hoc subgroup analysis of the CABANA RCT; N=1130 with baseline left atrial diameter (LAD) data; multicenter, AF patients randomized to ablation vs. drug therapy
- Primary outcome: Composite of death, disabling stroke, serious bleeding, or cardiac arrest — HR 0.30 (95% CI 0.11–0.78) for LAD ≤40 mm vs. HR 0.92 (95% CI 0.55–1.54) for LAD >40 mm; interaction P=.035
- Limitations: Post-hoc subgroup analysis of roughly half the CABANA population (those with available LAD data), limiting causal inference and increasing risk of spurious interaction findings

Effectiveness and safety of endovascular therapy compared to intravenous thrombolysis in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusions: a real-world multicentre study from the Italian SITS registry. *European Stroke Journal*. [41904677](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41904677/)

**Research Question:** Does endovascular therapy (with or without IVT) improve 90-day functional outcomes compared to IVT alone in acute ischaemic stroke due to medium-vessel occlusion in routine clinical practice?

EVT did not improve functional independence over IVT alone in MeVO stroke (60.6% vs 60.9%; OR 0.99) but increased haemorrhagic complications (18.4% vs 11.2%), reinforcing a cautious approach to routine EVT in this population.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective multicentre registry study (82 Italian SITS centres, 2020–2023), N=780 after 1:1 propensity score matching (390 per group); MeVO defined as ACA A1/A2, MCA M2/M3 or more distal, or PCA P1/P2
- Primary outcome: Functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days — 60.6% EVT±IVT vs 60.9% IVT alone (OR 0.99, 95% CI 0.73–1.34; P=.939)
- Limitations: Retrospective registry design with incomplete procedural data and anatomical heterogeneity in MeVO classification limits causal inference and generalisability across vessel subtypes

Efficacy and Safety of DOACs in Patients with Atrial Fibrillation and History of Falls or Risk of Falls: The Liverpool AF-Falls Project. A Systematic Review and Bayesian Network Meta-analysis. *Drugs & Aging*. [41845167](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41845167/)

**Research Question:** Which DOAC provides the best balance of stroke prevention and bleeding safety in AF patients with a history of or risk for falls?

Across all DOACs, apixaban ranked highest for stroke/systemic embolism reduction (HR 0.72 vs. VKA) while edoxaban ranked highest for major bleeding reduction (HR 0.66 vs. VKA), supporting DOAC use over VKA in fall-risk AF patients but suggesting agent choice matters.

**Details:**
- Design: Bayesian network meta-analysis of 10 studies (5 RCTs, 5 observational), AF patients with fall history or fall risk
- Primary outcome: Stroke/systemic embolism — apixaban HR 0.72 (95% CrI 0.59–0.96), rivaroxaban HR 0.80 (95% CrI 0.63–0.99); Major bleeding — edoxaban HR 0.66 (95% CrI 0.50–0.92), apixaban HR 0.67 (95% CrI 0.55–0.87)
- Limitations: Moderate-to-serious risk of bias across included studies; only 10 articles met inclusion criteria, limiting precision, particularly for edoxaban and dabigatran estimates
