**Acute Treatment**

Safety and efficacy of direct versus conventional transfer to angiography suite in patients with severe acute stroke treated with thrombectomy (DIRECT ANGIO) in France: a multicentre, open-label, blinded-endpoint, randomised controlled trial. *The Lancet Neurology*. [41864232](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41864232/)

**Research Question:** Does bypassing conventional CT imaging and transferring suspected LVO stroke patients directly to the angiography suite improve functional outcomes compared with the standard imaging-first pathway?

Direct transfer to the angiography suite was associated with a significantly increased risk of symptomatic intracranial haemorrhage (15% vs 0%; aOR 11.0) and no improvement in 90-day functional independence (36% vs 42%), leading to early trial termination — this strategy should not be adopted in clinical practice without further evidence.

**Details:**
- Design: Multicentre open-label RCT, N=115 (DTAS n=57, conventional n=58), ten French comprehensive stroke centres, patients ≤85 years with ASND-LVO admitted within 5 hours
- Primary outcome: mRS 0–2 at 90 days — 36% DTAS vs 42% conventional (aOR 0.73, 95% CI 0.32–1.69); no significant difference
- Limitations: Trial stopped early for safety after enrolling only 115 of the planned sample, resulting in wide confidence intervals that preclude definitive conclusions on both efficacy and the magnitude of harm

**Acute Treatment**

Effect of a clinical decision support system on stroke care quality and outcomes in patients with acute ischaemic stroke (GOLDEN BRIDGE II): cluster randomised clinical trial. *BMJ (Clinical research ed.)*. [41862204](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41862204/)

**Research Question:** Does an AI-assisted clinical decision support system (CDSS) providing imaging analysis, stroke classification, and evidence-based treatment recommendations reduce recurrent vascular events in patients with acute ischaemic stroke?

A CDSS integrating AI imaging analysis and evidence-based recommendations reduced 3-month recurrent vascular events from 3.9% to 2.9% (aHR 0.74) across 77 Chinese hospitals — a significant and sustained benefit that should accelerate adoption of structured decision support in acute stroke care.

**Details:**
- Design: Cluster randomised trial, 77 hospitals (38 intervention, 39 control), N = 21,603 patients with acute ischaemic stroke admitted within 7 days of onset, China, 2021–2023
- Primary outcome: New vascular event at 3 months — 2.9% (320/11,054) vs. 3.9% (416/10,549); aHR 0.74 (95% CI 0.58–0.93), P = 0.01; benefit sustained at 12 months (4.0% vs. 5.5%, aHR 0.73)
- Limitations: All hospitals were in China, limiting generalisability to settings with different baseline care quality and health infrastructure; no significant reduction in disability or all-cause mortality was demonstrated

**Epidemiology**

Stroke in China: advances in prevention and management on the path to a healthy China. *The Lancet Neurology*. [41864235](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41864235/)

**Research Question:** What progress has been made in stroke prevention and management in China over the past decade, and where do critical gaps remain?

Despite falling stroke incidence and mortality in China, rising prevalence driven by population ageing means the burden is far from controlled — and a nationwide push toward stroke centre networks, reperfusion therapies, and early blood pressure lowering after haemorrhage is shaping one of the world's largest stroke systems.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review, national scope, Chinese population
- Primary outcome: Stroke incidence and mortality have decreased over the past decade; prevalence continues to rise due to population ageing; high-quality RCT evidence supports reperfusion for ischaemic stroke, early BP lowering post-ICH, and individualised secondary prevention
- Limitations: Narrative review without systematic methodology; findings reflect a single country's health system and may not be generalisable; reliance on national registry and trial data subject to heterogeneity across regions

**Imaging**

Impact of Basal Ganglia Perivascular Spaces on Ischemic and Hemorrhagic Risks in Patients Taking Antithrombotic Therapies. *Neurology*. [41861278](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41861278/)

**Research Question:** In patients on antithrombotic therapy for cerebrovascular or cardiovascular disease, does the burden of basal ganglia enlarged perivascular spaces (BGPVSs) predict future ischemic stroke or major bleeding?

Severe BGPVS burden (≥21) was independently associated with a fourfold increase in major bleeding and a 2.6-fold increase in ischemic stroke, suggesting these MRI findings could flag patients at simultaneous high risk for both complications — a critical challenge for antithrombotic decision-making.

**Details:**
- Design: Prospective multicenter observational study, N = 5,065, patients with cerebrovascular or cardiovascular disease on antithrombotic therapy across 52 Japanese hospitals (2016–2019)
- Primary outcome: BGPVS ≥21 associated with major bleeding (aHR 4.04, 95% CI 1.17–13.92) and ischemic stroke (aHR 2.58, 95% CI 1.21–5.50); no significant association with intracranial hemorrhage alone or mortality
- Limitations: Median follow-up of only 2 years limits detection of longer-term events, and the observational design precludes causal inference about whether modifying antithrombotic therapy based on BGPVS burden would alter outcomes

**Prevention**

CTA-Based Plaque-RADS to Assess Etiologic Role of Nonstenotic Carotid Plaques in Patients With Embolic Stroke of Undetermined Source. *Neurology*. [41843860](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41843860/)

**Research Question:** Can a standardized CTA-based Plaque-RADS classification identify nonstenotic carotid plaques as a distinct causal stroke mechanism in patients with embolic stroke of undetermined source?

In ESUS patients, ipsilateral high-risk nonstenotic carotid plaques — identifiable reliably on routine CTA — were associated with a 3-fold increase in ipsilateral stroke recurrence and were inversely associated with competing cardioembolic sources, supporting them as a genuine causal mechanism that should drive secondary prevention decisions.

**Details:**
- Design: Single-center retrospective/prospective cohort study, N=512, consecutive ESUS patients with unilateral anterior circulation stroke and neck CTA
- Primary outcome: Ipsilateral high-risk Plaque-RADS (categories 3–4) was present in 21.3% of patients and was more common ipsilaterally than contralaterally (21.3% vs 11.9%; aOR 2.65, 95% CI 1.73–4.06); ipsilateral recurrence was 11.2% vs 3.0% (aSHR 3.19, 95% CI 1.15–8.83, p=0.026)
- Limitations: Single-center design limits generalizability, and the absence of randomized treatment data means the recurrence association cannot yet inform a specific antiplatelet or anticoagulation strategy

**Imaging**

Evaluation of Supracardiac Atherosclerosis in Stroke with a Noncontrast Head-Neck-Aortic Vessel Wall MRI. *Radiology*. [41842667](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41842667/)

**Research Question:** Can a 15-minute noncontrast head-neck-aortic vessel wall MRI protocol outperform supra-aortic CTA in detecting culprit plaques and reclassifying embolic stroke of undetermined source (ESUS)?

A single noncontrast vessel wall MRI scan covering the full supracardiac territory detected more plaques than CTA (81.5% vs 69.4%) and reclassified 42% of ESUS cases, cutting the undetermined stroke fraction from 35% to 20%.

**Details:**
- Design: Prospective single-center comparative imaging study, N=108, mean age 56 years, acute ischemic stroke or TIA, both modalities performed in each participant
- Primary outcome: vwMRI detected plaques in 88/108 (81.5%) vs CTA in 75/108 (69.4%), P=.002; ESUS reclassified from 38 to 22 participants (35.2% → 20.4%, P<.001); intraplaque hemorrhage identified in 27.8% by vwMRI (not assessable by CTA)
- Limitations: Single-center, short recruitment window (5 months), predominantly male cohort (82%), no histopathological validation of plaque characterization, and reclassification was imaging-based without long-term clinical outcome follow-up

**Imaging**

Cerebrovascular CTA radiomics for objective collateral grading in acute ischemic stroke. *European Radiology Experimental*. [41838258](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41838258/)

**Research Question:** Can a fully automated CTA radiomics pipeline objectively grade collateral circulation in acute large vessel occlusion as accurately as—or better than—existing methods?

A radiomics model combining vessel-tree and circle of Willis features achieved AUROC 0.87 on internal and 0.83 on external testing, outperforming the standard atlas-based MCA mask approach (AUROC 0.82 internal, 0.66 external), suggesting it could replace subjective rater-dependent collateral grading in thrombectomy decision-making.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective radiomics study; training/validation n = 274, internal test n = 69 (MR CLEAN trial), external validation n = 140 acute LVO patients
- Primary outcome: AUROC for classifying sufficient vs. insufficient collaterals (Tan score >50% vs. ≤50%); combined vessel-tree/CoW model AUROC 0.88 (internal) and 0.87 (combined features, external 0.83)
- Limitations: Retrospective design using a single historical trial (MR CLEAN) for training limits generalizability to contemporary thrombectomy-era imaging protocols; binary collateral classification (sufficient vs. insufficient) may oversimplify clinically relevant gradations
