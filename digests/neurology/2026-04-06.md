**Stroke**

Left Atrial Appendage Closure or Anticoagulation for Atrial Fibrillation. *New England Journal of Medicine*. [41910347](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910347/)

**Research Question:** In atrial fibrillation patients who are eligible for anticoagulation, does left atrial appendage closure (LAAC) offer a viable alternative to NOACs for preventing stroke while reducing bleeding risk?

In anticoagulation-eligible AF patients, LAAC was noninferior to NOACs for the composite of cardiovascular death, stroke, or systemic embolism and cut non-procedure-related bleeding nearly in half — making it a compelling option for patients where bleeding risk is a dominant concern.

**Details:**
- Design: Prospective, international, randomized trial; N=3000; AF patients suitable for anticoagulation, mean CHA₂DS₂-VASc 3.5
- Primary outcome: Efficacy composite (CV death, stroke, systemic embolism) at 3 years: 5.7% (LAAC) vs. 4.8% (NOAC); difference 0.9 percentage points (95% CI −0.8 to 2.6); P<0.001 for noninferiority. Non-procedure-related bleeding: 10.9% vs. 19.0% (HR 0.55; 95% CI 0.45–0.67); P<0.001 for superiority.
- Limitations: The trial is ongoing, and 3-year follow-up may not capture late device-related complications or delayed stroke risk; procedure-related adverse events are excluded from the bleeding comparison, which could understate early harms of LAAC.

**Stroke**

Intensive LDL Cholesterol Targeting in Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease. *New England Journal of Medicine*. [41910315](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41910315/)

**Research Question:** In patients with atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease on secondary prevention, does targeting LDL <55 mg/dL reduce major cardiovascular events compared to the conventional target of <70 mg/dL?

Targeting LDL <55 mg/dL reduced the composite cardiovascular endpoint by one-third versus <70 mg/dL (6.6% vs. 9.7%; HR 0.67), supporting more aggressive LDL lowering in atherosclerotic disease — including stroke patients who are a core population in this trial.

**Details:**
- Design: Open-label randomized superiority trial, N=3,048, patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease in South Korea
- Primary outcome: Composite of cardiovascular death, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, revascularization, or hospitalization for unstable angina at 3 years — 6.6% (intensive) vs. 9.7% (conventional); HR 0.67 (95% CI 0.52–0.86; P=0.002)
- Limitations: Open-label design introduces performance bias; single-country (South Korean) cohort limits generalizability; the achieved LDL separation between arms was modest (56 vs. 66 mg/dL), and stroke-specific subgroup data are not reported in the abstract

Urogenital dysfunction in neurological diseases. *Nature Reviews Neurology*. [41927939](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41927939/)

**Research Question:** How does neural injury across the neuraxis produce urogenital dysfunction in common neurological conditions, and what management strategies are supported by current evidence?

Urogenital dysfunction—spanning overactive/underactive bladder and sexual dysfunction—occurs across virtually all major neurological diseases and this review consolidates the underlying physiology, condition-specific patterns, and current management approaches into a single reference for clinicians.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review; no trial-level N; covers stroke, Alzheimer disease, white matter disease, normal-pressure hydrocephalus, Parkinson disease, dementia with Lewy bodies, multiple system atrophy, spinal cord injury, multiple sclerosis, and spina bifida
- Primary outcome: Descriptive synthesis of urogenital dysfunction mechanisms and management across CNS and spinal cord disorders; no pooled effect sizes reported
- Limitations: Narrative rather than systematic review with no meta-analytic data; evidence quality and comparability across the conditions covered is inherently heterogeneous

Large-scale proteomics across neurological disorders uncovers biomarker panel and targets in multiple sclerosis. *Cell*. [41747728](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41747728/)

**Research Question:** Can large-scale CSF proteomics identify a validated, clinically compatible biomarker panel that distinguishes MS from other inflammatory neurological diseases and supports disease staging?

A 22-protein CSF panel outperformed established MS markers in diagnostically challenging cases and was validated by targeted mass spectrometry in an independent cohort, with proteome-based staging correlating with clinical outcomes across the relapsing-progressive spectrum.

**Details:**
- Design: Cross-sectional proteomics study; ~5,000 individuals across multiple neurological disorders for the broad characterization phase; MS-focused analysis used improved workflow quantifying ~2,100 proteins per sample

- Limitations: Predominantly cross-sectional design limits causal inference; clinical translation requires prospective validation of the targeted assay in routine diagnostic settings before the panel can replace or formally supplement current biomarkers

**Stroke**

Left Atrial Appendage Closure or Medical Therapy in Atrial Fibrillation. *New England Journal of Medicine*. [41849741](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41849741/)

**Research Question:** In atrial fibrillation patients at high risk for both stroke and bleeding, is left atrial appendage closure noninferior to physician-directed best medical therapy for preventing stroke, embolism, major bleeding, or cardiovascular death?

Left atrial appendage closure failed to meet noninferiority versus optimized medical therapy in high-risk AF patients, with a numerically higher event rate in the device group — meaning this procedure cannot currently be recommended as a routine alternative to anticoagulation for stroke prevention.

**Details:**
- Design: Multicenter randomized equivalence trial (CLOSURE-AF), N=888 analyzed, elderly AF patients in Germany (mean age 77.9 years, mean CHA₂DS₂-VASc 5.2, mean HAS-BLED 3.0)
- Primary outcome: Composite of stroke, systemic embolism, major bleeding, or cardiovascular/unexplained death — 16.8 vs. 13.3 events per 100 patient-years (device vs. medical therapy); noninferiority not met (P=0.44)
- Limitations: Single-country trial limiting generalizability; physician-directed "best medical care" allowed DOAC use, meaning the comparator reflects optimized rather than real-world treatment, likely raising the bar for noninferiority

**Stroke**

Current Management of Cerebral Venous Thrombosis. *Stroke*. [41924888](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41924888/)

**Research Question:** How should cerebral venous thrombosis be managed across its full disease continuum, from acute treatment through long-term secondary prevention and chronic sequelae?

DOACs appear comparable to vitamin K antagonists for most CVT patients, but optimal treatment duration remains uncertain, and long-term morbidity from fatigue, headache, cognitive disturbance, and epilepsy is substantial even among patients who achieve functional independence.

**Details:**
- Design: Narrative review; no primary N
- Primary outcome: Anticoagulation (including when intracranial hemorrhage is present) remains the cornerstone of acute treatment; endovascular therapy and decompressive craniectomy reserved for severe refractory cases; highest recurrence risk in cancer, antiphospholipid antibody syndrome, prior VTE, and idiopathic CVT
- Limitations: Evidence gaps persist across the disease continuum; most recommendations extrapolated from small or observational studies rather than large RCTs

**Multiple Sclerosis**

Diagnostic Value of the Kappa Free Light Chain Index to Distinguish MOGAD, NMOSD, and MS. *Neurology*. [41921124](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41921124/)

**Research Question:** Can the CSF kappa free light chain index (KFLC-Index) reliably differentiate MS from MOGAD and AQP4-NMOSD, and does it outperform or add value to oligoclonal bands?

At a threshold of 5.7, KFLC-Index distinguished MOGAD from MS with 89% sensitivity and 89% specificity (AUC 0.93), and combining it with oligoclonal bands outperformed either test alone — making it a practical addition to the diagnostic workup when these conditions are difficult to separate.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective multicenter case-control study, N=303 (140 MOGAD, 37 AQP4-NMOSD, 126 MS), 18 French centers
- Primary outcome: KFLC-Index AUC 0.93 (95% CI 0.90–0.96) for MOGAD vs. MS; AUC 0.82 for NMOSD vs. MS; AUC 0.77 for MOGAD vs. NMOSD; median KFLC-Index was 0.60 in MOGAD, 8.85 in NMOSD, and 49.90 in MS
- Limitations: Retrospective design with MS comparators drawn from a single center, introducing potential selection bias and limiting generalizability

**Multiple Sclerosis**

Long-term disability after initiation of platform versus high-efficacy disease-modifying therapy in relapsing-onset multiple sclerosis. *Journal of Neurology*. [41920324](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41920324/)

**Research Question:** Does the disability benefit of high-efficacy DMTs over platform therapies in relapsing MS operate through reduction of relapse-associated worsening, progression independent of relapse activity, or both?

High-efficacy DMTs cut relapse-associated worsening by 40% and halved the risk of reaching EDSS 3–4, but had no effect on progression independent of relapse activity — meaning current therapies leave a major driver of long-term disability untouched.

**Details:**
- Design: Observational cohort, N = 2,563 DMT-naïve relapsing-onset MS patients; population-based Swedish MS registry, 40 clinics, 2005–2019
- Primary outcome: High-efficacy DMT vs. platform — RAW HR 0.60 (95% CI 0.38–0.92); PIRA HR 1.05 (95% CI 0.79–1.39); EDSS 3 HR 0.26 (95% CI 0.17–0.38); EDSS 4 HR 0.32 (95% CI 0.18–0.54)
- Limitations: Follow-up censored at treatment switch or discontinuation, introducing informative censoring bias — patients who switched from platform to high-efficacy therapy are removed, likely underestimating the platform group's long-term disability accumulation and potentially inflating the apparent benefit of sustained high-efficacy treatment

**General Neurology**

Sonographic optic nerve sheath diameter measurement for detecting intracranial hypertension in a suspected population: a systematic review and meta-analysis of diagnostic thresholds and influencing factors. *Journal of Neurology*. [41925916](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41925916/)

**Research Question:** Can bedside ultrasound measurement of optic nerve sheath diameter reliably detect intracranial hypertension when invasive ICP monitoring is unavailable?

An ONSD threshold ≥5.1 mm identifies elevated ICP with clinically useful accuracy, and multiplanar technique cuts missed diagnoses by 50% — making this a viable bedside screening tool in emergencies, though multicenter validation and standardized protocols are still needed before routine adoption.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis, 8 databases searched through May 2025, prospective/retrospective/case-control studies pooled using a bivariate random-effects model
- Primary outcome: ONSD ≥5.1 mm identified as critical diagnostic threshold; multiplanar technique achieved LR− 0.1; nontraumatic etiology yielded sensitivity 91.9% and diagnostic odds ratio 63.3 vs. 30.5 for traumatic brain injury; 25 cmH₂O ICP reference threshold produced specificity 88.4% and LR+ 6.5
- Limitations: Significant heterogeneity across measurement techniques, ICP reference thresholds (20 mmHg vs. 25 cmH₂O), and populations (Asian vs. non-Asian); no multicenter external validation of the 5.1 mm cutoff; standardized measurement protocols do not yet exist

**Research Question:** Do complement inhibitors and FcRn blockers provide clinically meaningful, durable benefit in adults with AChR antibody-positive generalized myasthenia gravis compared to placebo or standard care?

Both drug classes significantly improved function and quality of life in AChR-Ab⁺ gMG — more than doubling the odds of meaningful clinical response — with complement inhibitors additionally showing sustained benefit to 3 years and a 30% corticosteroid reduction rate.

**Details:**

Efficacy and safety of disease-modifying oral drugs in treatment of relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis: systematic review and network meta-analysis. *Frontiers in Immunology*. [41918730](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41918730/)

**Research Question:** Among available oral disease-modifying therapies for RRMS, which agents offer the best combination of relapse reduction and tolerability?

Siponimod 2 mg ranked highest for annualized relapse rate reduction across six active oral DMTs, but the safety leader for discontinuation due to adverse events was a non-standard fingolimod dose (0.25 mg) that should not guide prescribing.

**Details:**
- Design: Network meta-analysis of RCTs, N = 14,869, adult RRMS patients across 15 trials
- Primary outcome: Annualized relapse rate — Siponimod 2 mg ranked best (SUCRA 86.9%); Laquinimod 0.3 mg ranked worst (SUCRA 10.1%); for adverse event–related discontinuation, Fingolimod 0.25 mg ranked best (SUCRA 83.1%) and Siponimod 10 mg ranked worst (SUCRA 3.1%)
- Limitations: The tolerability "winner" (fingolimod 0.25 mg) is an off-label exploratory dose, and the network includes only 15 RCTs with heterogeneous comparator arms, limiting the reliability of indirect comparisons

Performance of the 2024 McDonald diagnostic criteria for multiple sclerosis. *Multiple Sclerosis*. [41776382](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41776382/)

**Research Question:** Do the 2024 McDonald criteria improve diagnostic sensitivity and speed compared to prior iterations in patients presenting with CIS or RIS?

The 2024 McDonald criteria diagnosed MS in 81.4% of patients versus 74.9% with 2017 criteria, cutting median time to diagnosis from 2.1 to 2.0 months — and prospectively captured 85% at baseline, suggesting meaningful real-world gains in early detection.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective cohort with prospective validation arm; N=163 (plus 13 prospective); CIS/RIS patients aged 18–50, seen within 6 months of symptom onset, 2013–2022

- Limitations: Single-center study with a relatively small sample (N=163), limiting generalizability; retrospective application of newer criteria to older cohorts introduces ascertainment bias

**Dementia & Cognition**

Risk of Alzheimer Dementia After High-Dose vs Standard-Dose Influenza Vaccination. *Neurology*. [41921123](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41921123/)

**Research Question:** Does high-dose influenza vaccine (H-IIV) confer greater protection against incident Alzheimer dementia than standard-dose (S-IIV) in adults ≥65 years?

High-dose influenza vaccination was associated with a significantly lower risk of Alzheimer dementia compared with standard-dose vaccination over 25 months (minimum NNT = 185), with a stronger and more sustained effect in women — suggesting vaccine dose may matter for dementia prevention, not just flu prevention.

**Details:**
- Design: Retrospective cohort study using sequential nested target-trial emulation; H-IIV n = 120,775 (185,183 person-trials), S-IIV n = 44,022 (53,918 person-trials); US claims data 2014–2019, age ≥65, no prior cognitive impairment
- Primary outcome: Significantly lower AD risk with H-IIV during months 1–25 postvaccination; minimum NNT = 185.2 at 25 months; effect persisted longer in women (months 1–13, NNT = 416.7) than men (months 17–24, significant only in ITT analysis, NNT = 232.6)
- Limitations: Follow-up capped at 3 years; no sociodemographic, lifestyle, biomarker, or mortality data; claims-based AD definition; mechanism (infection-mediated vs. direct immunological) unknown

**Research Question:** Does adjunctive intra-arterial tenecteplase after successful endovascular thrombectomy improve functional outcomes without increasing hemorrhagic risk in large vessel occlusion stroke?

Adding intra-arterial tenecteplase after successful EVT increases the rate of excellent functional outcome (mRS 0–1) by 8 percentage points (NNT 13) with no significant rise in symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage or mortality, suggesting a viable strategy to address futile recanalization.

**Details:**
- Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized and non-randomized clinical trials; number of included studies not reported in abstract; population: AIS patients with LVO achieving successful recanalization post-EVT
- Primary outcome: mRS 0–1 rate — RD 0.08 (95% CI 0.02–0.14, p = 0.004; I² = 0%); sICH RD 0.02 (95% CI −0.01–0.05, p = 0.13); mortality RD −0.02 (p = 0.36)
- Limitations: Evidence certainty graded via GRADE (level not specified in abstract); inclusion of non-randomized studies alongside RCTs and variable TNK dosing strategies across subgroups limits confidence in pooled estimates
